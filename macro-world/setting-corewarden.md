# Core Warden — Setting Bible

## The World of Matlu

The game takes place in **Matlu**, a world where multiple realities are beginning to collide. The player experiences this through the lens of a single kingdom — the Wildkin beastkin homeland — as it faces threats from every direction simultaneously, none of which it fully understands.

## The Beastkin Kingdom (primary setting)

**Geography:** A strip of habitable land between a mountain range and the ocean. Forests, meadows, rivers, and wetlands. Temperate climate. The kingdom has never been large — it doesn't need to be. The mountains and the sea have always been its walls.

**Society:** Governed by the Highfang and the Great Clans council (see Wildkin race lore). An old, stable civilization with internal political tensions (the Clan Question) but no recent wars. Isolationist by geography, not ideology — they simply haven't had reason to look outward.

**History of confinement:** The Wildkin were not always settled. The older clans remember when packs roamed freely, following game and season. Dens were temporary. But squeezed between mountains and sea, with human kingdoms pressing the eastern border, the territory shrank generation by generation. Roaming became harder, then impractical, then impossible. The Great Clans formalized because someone had to manage who lived where when "everywhere" stopped being an option. Human influence crept in through proximity — permanent foundations, rectangular buildings, latched doors, fences, market squares. The oldest buildings still look like dens. The newest ones have iron hinges. Whether this is evolution or erosion is the quiet argument that runs through every settlement.

**The ocean:** Unexplored. There are no sea-faring beastkin. No one has ever gone far from shore. The ocean is a wall, not a road. This may change.

## The Borders

### West — The Mountains

A tall, old range that has always marked the edge of the known world. The beastkin have trails through the lower passes but have never pushed deep.

**The Dvergr (Dwarves):** Turns out they've been inside the mountains the entire time. They're only now appearing on the beastkin side — walking out of tunnels that weren't there last season, trading at pass-towns, asking questions about the surface. Nobody knows why they're emerging *now*. The dwarves themselves seem reluctant to explain. Some are friendly. Some are clearly fleeing something.

**The Undead:** In the higher passes, undead have appeared. Recent. Within the last year. Not an army — scattered, confused, hostile. They come from deeper in the mountains. The beastkin don't have a framework for undead — their dead stay dead. This is new and frightening. Possibly connected to whatever is driving the dwarves out.

### East — The Human Lands

On the far side of a border region (forest, river, or lowland gap — TBD), a human empire or federation of kingdoms. The exact political structure has varied over the centuries — sometimes a unified empire, sometimes squabbling kingdoms, sometimes a republic. Currently trending toward consolidation under a militaristic leadership.

**Historical relations:** Up and down. Trade periods, border skirmishes, a few real wars, long stretches of mutual ignoring. The beastkin are not conquerors and the humans have always had easier expansion directions. Currently: relations are deteriorating. Border incidents increasing. Human rhetoric about beastkin growing harsher.

**The corruption angle:** Unknown to anyone on either side, the worsening relations are being influenced by corruption — the same force that's causing problems everywhere. Human leaders are becoming more aggressive, more suspicious, more paranoid. Beastkin border clans are becoming more defensive, more hostile. Neither side realizes they're being pushed.

### Below — The Underworld

Beneath the kingdom, something is stirring. Strange creatures are surfacing through cave systems, old wells, cracked earth. These are not native to Matlu.

**Spinolandet connection:** This is the world of Spinolandet bleeding through — a bio-organic alien realm (Zerg + dinosaur + organic alien aesthetic) that exists in a parallel layer. The merger is not intentional from either side. The creatures are as confused as the beastkin. Some are hostile by nature, some are hostile because they're terrified.

**Human ruins:** Mixed in with the Spinolandet emergence are old human structures — ruins, foundations, tunnels — that predate the beastkin kingdom. These suggest that humans (or something human-like) were here before the beastkin, which raises uncomfortable questions about who the "native" inhabitants really are.

### The Ocean — Unknown

The sea has always been there. The beastkin fish from shore, harvest shellfish, build no boats. There is no Wildkin (Sea) clan. No legends of sea voyages. The ocean is simply the edge.

What's out there? Nobody knows. Yet.

## The Corruption

The connective thread. Corruption is:
- Worsening human-beastkin relations (political manipulation)
- Possibly driving the dwarves to the surface (underground corruption?)
- Possibly raising the undead in the mountain passes
- Possibly causing the Spinolandet bleed-through (weakening the barrier between worlds)

The player's role as Core Warden is to understand and fight this corruption. They don't know any of the above connections at the start. They experience each threat as separate and unrelated. The realization that it's all one force is the arc.

## What the Player Knows at Start

Almost nothing. They are in a beastkin settlement. Things are wrong — creatures from below, strangers from the mountains, bad news from the human border. The local clans are worried but not panicked. The Highfang's council is debating. Nobody has answers.

## Design Notes

- The kingdom is small enough to be one game map — mountains to ocean, human border to wilderness
- Each border threat is a gameplay zone with its own enemy types and narrative
- The internal politics (Clan Question) provides NPC dialogue texture, not a separate quest line
- The corruption is the unifying mechanic but the player discovers the connections, not told them
- "Yet" is a deliberate word — the ocean, the deeper mountains, the human lands are all future expansion directions
