# Matlu — World Bible

This document is the creative foundation for all lore writing in Matlu.
AI agents generating lore entries should read this fully before creating anything.

---

## Core premise

The player is a cleansing force moving through a world being consumed by corruption.
Corruption does not just destroy — it transforms, accelerates, and reveals what was already
wrong. On Earth, corruption speeds up a collapse that was already coming. On the alien worlds,
it mutates ecosystems that were already violent and strange.

The tone begins **cozy and familiar** and darkens with each world and each level.
The player should feel they have something to lose before they feel they are losing it.

---

## Core themes

- **Corruption as accelerant** — It does not create evil from nothing. It finds what is already
  fragile and broken and pushes it faster toward ruin. A struggling ecosystem collapses. A tense
  society fractures. A wounded creature becomes a predator.
- **The cost of cleansing** — Cleansing is not painless or clean. Something is lost each time,
  even when the right choice is made. Neutral kills carry weight.
- **Nature versus control** — Across all three worlds, the tension is between wild, living systems
  and the forces (corporate, mechanical, corrupted) that try to dominate or harvest them.
- **Small things matter** — A rabbit warren. A pond. A settlement at a forest edge.
  The game is won or lost in the accumulation of small acts, not one heroic moment.

---

## Writing style

- **Grounded first, then strange.** Start every description from something the reader recognises —
  an animal behaviour, a landscape feature, a human habit — then let the corruption or alienness
  emerge from that familiar base.
- **Spare prose.** Short sentences. No purple language. Let strangeness carry itself.
- **Specificity over generality.** "A fox with glass-white eyes that does not blink" beats
  "a corrupted fox with unusual eyes."
- **No omniscient narrator.** Write as an observer who has been in this world — someone with
  incomplete knowledge, not a god describing a game mechanic.
- **Avoid:** chosen one framing, cartoonish evil, exposition dumps, explaining what corruption
  is directly. Show what it does.

## The corruption

The corruption has no face and no agenda. It is a pressure — like heat, like gravity — that
finds weakness and amplifies it. Its origin is unknown within the game world. Characters have
theories; none are confirmed.

Corrupted entities are not evil versions of themselves. They are **extreme versions** — more
territorial, more hungry, more afraid, more aggressive. A corrupted fox is not a demon. It is
a fox whose fear response never turns off.

Visually: corruption manifests as **distortion at the edges**. Fur that grows the wrong direction.
Eyes that catch light they shouldn't. Movement that is slightly too fast, or that pauses in
wrong places. Colours that are almost right but not quite — a fox that is too orange, a crow
that is too black, water that is too still.

## Earth

### What it is

Earth is the player's home world. Level 1 takes place here. The landscape is a northern European
coastline — cold light, forest edges meeting the sea, small human settlements at river mouths and
valley floors. Familiar animals: foxes, crows, rabbits, deer, seabirds.

The player arrives when corruption has only just begun. Things are still recognisable. This is
deliberate — the player needs to feel at home before they feel the wrongness.

### The arc: contemporary → Mad Max + Cyberpunk

Earth does not hold. The corruption accelerates societal fracture that was already underway.
What might have taken a century of slow collapse happens in years or months. Lore entries
should reflect **where on this arc** they sit.

**Early / contemporary (Level 1):**
- Recognisable modern-day northern Europe
- Small towns, fishing communities, mixed forests
- Wildlife is abundant and mostly uncorrupted
- People are anxious but functional
- Factions: municipal authorities, conservation groups, local clans beginning to form at the edges
- Tone: cozy unease. Something is wrong but no one can name it.

**Mid collapse (implied, off-screen during Level 1):**
- Supply chains failing. Fuel scarce. Grid unreliable.
- Settlements begin to wall themselves. Some go dark.
- Corporate emergency contractors arrive with their own agenda.
- Corruption zones spreading. Wildlife fleeing or transforming.
- Tone: desperate improvisation. Mad Max aesthetics beginning to show — salvaged vehicles,
  armed checkpoints, barter replacing currency.

**Late / dystopia (future levels on Earth, or background lore):**
- Wasteland zones alternate with fortified corporate enclaves
- The enclaves have power, neon, surveillance, synthetic food. The wasteland has none of it.
- Clan societies have formed with their own rituals around the corruption — some worship it,
  some fight it, some harvest it.
- Corporate entities are extracting something from corrupted zones. Nobody outside knows what.
- Tone: high-tech cruelty alongside desperate survival. Cyberpunk aesthetics fully present —
  not chrome and rain, but solar panels on ruined rooftops and data pads on salvaged trucks.

### Factions (Earth)

**The Settlement Wardens** — loose alliance of town leaders trying to maintain pre-collapse
norms. Bureaucratic, well-meaning, increasingly irrelevant. Their language is still municipal.

**Scavenger clans** — formed from people who left or were expelled from settlements.
Highly mobile, tribal, pragmatic. Not villains — they are survivors. Suspicious of outsiders
and of anything that smells like authority.

**Meridian Logistics** — a corporate entity operating under emergency contracts.
They move supplies and they take payment in land rights and data. Polite, professional,
and not to be trusted. Their presence correlates with things getting worse.

**The Watchers** — a loose network of people who understood corruption before anyone else did.
No central leadership. Share information through coded channels. Some are scientists. Some are
not. They know things they are not saying.

### Writing Earth lore

When writing Earth entries, ground them in the **contemporary layer first** and let the
dystopian direction show as subtext or future implication — unless the entry is explicitly
set in the mid-collapse or late periods.

A fox living near a settlement in Level 1 does not know about Meridian Logistics.
It knows about bins and compost heaps and the smell of the sea.
Start there.

## Spinolandet

### What it is

Spinolandet is a world that lives underground. The surface is not dead — but it is secondary.
Something happened to this world's star, or its orbit, or its atmosphere, long enough ago
that the memory is geological. The sky delivers radiation in spectrums that surface life
never fully adapted to. What lives above ground does so at cost: heavy chitin, photophobic
eyes, short seasons of breeding before retreating. Most of the world's complexity is below.

The tunnels are enormous. Some of them were made.

### The Precursors

There was once a civilisation here. Not like Earth's — no cities in the human sense, no
written language anyone has decoded. What they built was biological. They shaped living
things the way other civilisations shape stone: deliberately, over generations, with
something that looked like intention. Their architecture was grown. Their tools were
organisms. Their infrastructure was a network of modified organisms that processed,
filtered, transported, stored. DNA was their engineering language.

They are gone. Not extinct in a dramatic sense — there is no evidence of a war or a
cataclysm. They simply stop appearing in the fossil record. The most coherent theory,
for those few in the game world who study such things, is that they modified themselves
out of sapience: optimised their own biology so thoroughly for underground survival that
whatever made them a civilisation got edited away. Their descendants are still here.
They are not people anymore.

What remains of their work is the ecosystem. Everything alive in Spinolandet is, in some
sense, a descendant of something they built or modified or cultivated. The current life
did not evolve from scratch — it evolved from engineering. Which is why Spinolandet's
biology feels purposeful in ways that natural selection alone does not produce.

### The creature design pillars

Four things to hold simultaneously when designing any Spinolandet organism:

**Scale and predatory logic (dinosaur)** — Spinolandet's megafauna operate at Cretaceous
scale. Apex predators that make a human feel like prey before they have done anything.
Bulk, aggression, territorial range measured in kilometres. Not monsters — large animals
doing what large animals do.

**Caste warfare (Starship Troopers arachnids)** — the dominant species groups are not
armies but biological systems. There are specialist forms: fast expendable runners,
armoured assault bodies, long-range projectors that discharge mana in bursts, and somewhere
behind them, coordinator organisms that the individual units serve without knowing it.
You can kill the warriors for a long time without denting the system. What you need to find
is what coordinates them — and what coordinates it is older and harder to reach.

**Evolutionary drive (Zerg)** — these organisms do not stay the same. Under enough
environmental pressure they change form. A colonial borer adapts to a new tunnel type
in three generations. A wounded apex predator reroutes blood to armour. The Precursors
designed adaptation itself: organisms that can remodel their own biology in response to
stress. The corruption accelerates this. Corrupted creatures do not just become more
aggressive — they change. A filter-feeder under corruption stress may not stay a
filter-feeder for long.

**Deep-sea body plans** — the deep tunnels have never seen light. Their fauna evolved
around pressure, vibration, chemical gradient, and mana-flux rather than vision.
Radial symmetry common in ceiling-dwelling filter-feeders. Tentacle structures
optimised for sensory reach. Bioluminescent lures that mimic the mana-glow of safe
organisms to attract prey. Some tunnel predators are organised around their sensory
array rather than their digestive system — anatomy that has no Earth analogue.
The eyes, where they exist, are vestigial or repurposed for something else.

### The ecosystem

Think of the Cretaceous — scale, aggression, the sense that everything is too large and
moving too fast — filtered through insect and deep-sea logic together. Colonies. Castes.
Exoskeleton over soft tissue. Things that communicate chemically, that sacrifice
individuals without hesitation, that build structures larger than themselves over
generations without any single organism understanding the whole. And underneath them,
in the lightless lower tunnels, organisms with no front, no eyes, no bilateral symmetry —
anchored to the ceiling, trailing sensor-filaments fifty metres long.

Bilateral symmetry is common but not universal. Some things have no clear front.
A creature's lifecycle may include stages that look like entirely different species —
the sessile filter-feeder anchored to a cavern wall is the juvenile form of something
that, given enough time and the right stress, will stand two metres tall and be
classified under a different family.

There are localized hive minds. A colony of tunnel-borers that shares sensory input
across its hundreds of bodies — not one consciousness, but something closer to a
distributed awareness. Each body acts individually when isolated, but in proximity they
synchronise: the same threat response runs through the whole group at once, the same
hunger, the same direction. Separating them causes distress that looks almost like grief.
These are not the planet's rulers. They are one solution among many to the problem of
coordination in the dark.

### Mana in Spinolandet

Mana exists here, but it is not magic in any sense that Vattenpandalandet would recognise.
It is a substrate — a property of the deep rock, of certain mineral formations, of the
water in the deepest tunnels. Living things in Spinolandet have incorporated it the way
Earth life incorporated iron: as a functional material, processed by specific organs,
used for specific purposes.

Some organisms generate bioluminescence via mana. Some have organs that detect it
directionally — a kind of mana-sense, useful in complete darkness. A few larger predators
can discharge it in short bursts, not as spellcasting but the way an electric eel discharges:
reflexively, as a weapon of last resort that costs them something.

There are no [Classes] here. No system. Mana is metabolic, not metaphysical.
The Precursors almost certainly understood it — their modifications show evidence of
deliberate mana-organ engineering. Whatever they knew died with their sapience.

### The corruption here

The corruption does not darken Spinolandet. It simplifies.

Spinolandet's ecosystems are deeply interdependent. The tunnel-borers aerate soil that
feeds fungal networks that feed the herbivore colonies that the predators cull.
A hive that dies takes three other species' populations with it over the following season.
Everything is connected in ways that took millions of years of post-Precursor evolution
to build.

Corruption severs those connections. Not violently — it just removes the cooperation.
A fungal network stops sharing nutrients and starts competing with its own nodes. A
predator that normally hunts selectively begins hunting everything it can reach. The
hive minds fragment: bodies that shared awareness start acting independently, without
coordination, consuming resources with no regard for the colony.

The result is not chaos but monoculture. Corrupted zones trend toward one dominant
organism that out-competes everything else — until there is nothing left to consume,
and it too dies. What the player is fighting in Spinolandet is not monsters.
It is the collapse of a system.

### The five enemy types

The specific enemy designs are pending sprite work. The design framework — one type per
pillar, with one deep-tunnel outlier — is:

| Type | Pillar | Behaviour |
|------|--------|-----------|
| **Runner** | Zerg / insect caste | Fast, expendable, swarms in numbers. The body the colony sends first. |
| **Brute** | Dinosaur scale | Slow, enormous, devastating on contact. Does not give ground. |
| **Projector** | Starship Troopers artillery | Discharges mana at range. Stays behind the runners. High priority target. |
| **Borer / colony unit** | Hive mind | Tunnel-specialist. Coordinates others passively. Killing it fragments nearby units. |
| **Lure** | Deep-sea ambush | Stationary or near-stationary. Mimics something safe. Revealed only on approach. |

What all five share:

- All are descended from Precursor bioengineering, however distantly
- All have mana-organs of some kind, even if vestigial
- None are evil. They are organisms operating under corrupted drives —
  a predator whose threat-response never resolves, a colonial organism
  whose coordination signal has gone into a loop, a filter-feeder whose
  intake has lost its off-switch
- Corrupted variants look almost right. The chitin is the wrong colour.
  The movement is slightly too fast, or too repetitive. The mana-glow
  pulses at an interval that is just slightly wrong
- Under corruption, they may change form — the runner becoming armoured,
  the filter-feeder standing up

### Writing Spinolandet lore

The voice here is closest to *Annihilation* — the observer is documenting something
they do not fully understand, and the documentation is slightly inadequate to the reality.
Do not try to explain the biology completely. Let it be almost-understood.

Write organisms the way a field researcher would: what it looks like, what it does,
what happens when you approach it. Not what it feels. These are not characters.
The Precursors are characters — but they are absent. Their absence is the emotional
register of this world.

The exception: the localized hive minds. Write those with something approaching
interiority — not human interiority, but the sense that the colony is experiencing
something. The grief of separation. The confusion of fragmentation.
That is where the emotional weight lives.

**Avoid:** naming Spinolandet creatures with fantasy naming conventions.
They should have field-designation energy — the kind of name a researcher would
give something they found last week and do not fully understand yet.

## Vattenpandalandet

### What it is

Vattenpandalandet is a world of stories and magic — a place where the conventions of fantasy
literature are not conventions but physics. Classes are real. Skills manifest as genuine
supernatural ability. Inns are sanctuaries. Ancient ruins contain things that should stay buried.
The seas are older than the civilisations that sail them.

Think of every fantasy novel you have ever read, every RPG world you have ever inhabited, layered
on top of each other until the seams show. That is Vattenpandalandet. It is a world that has
been through so many ages, so many heroes and apocalypses and rebirths, that it carries the weight
of all of them simultaneously. The past is not backstory here. It is active and present and
occasionally comes back to kill people.

### Myst — the world's living current

Myst is the flow of mana through the world — not a resource to be spent but an animating
principle, like weather, like tide. It moves through places and through people. Where it
runs strongly, magic works, stories hold, Skills mean something. Where it thins, things
become less than themselves.

The world's geography is a classic fantasy landscape: highlands and lowlands, ancient forests,
archipelagos where isolated peoples developed for centuries before trade connected them.
The land has its own character — valleys, mountain passes, coastal deltas, ruins — but it is
not defined by any single element. Water, wind, stone, mist: all of these carry Myst and are
carried by it. None of them own it.

**The Wandering Inn** by pirateaba is the primary creative reference for this world.
Read it as your bible for tone, species complexity, how to write a Skill activating,
how an inn should feel, and how to treat characters the world would call monsters.

### The system

Every sapient being in Vattenpandalandet has a Class and levels within it. [Innkeeper].
[Soldier]. [Fisherwoman]. [Bard]. Skills unlock at milestone levels — not mechanical bonuses,
but genuine magic that reflects who you are. A [Chef] who cooks out of love unlocks different
Skills than one who cooks for money.

The system rewards authenticity and punishes emptiness. It is also a trap. Entire societies
stratify by level. People define themselves by their Class and forget they were something before
it. The system's origins are unknown and possibly sinister — it was built, not grown, and
something built it for a reason nobody remembers.

Corruption interacts with the system directly. Corrupted individuals gain levels faster — and
lose the meaning behind their Skills. A [Healer] who is corrupted may keep the Skill but find
it works in reverse. An [Innkeeper] whose inn is corrupted finds the doors no longer bring
warmth — strangers who eat there leave as enemies. Corruption in Vattenpandalandet does not
destroy the system. It hollows it out and wears it like a costume.

### Runescript — the language of Myst

Magic in Vattenpandalandet is not cast. It is written, and then executed.

The language is called Runescript by scholars who study it and simply *the marks* by
everyone else. It uses the Elder Futhark rune alphabet as its glyphs — each of the
twenty-four runes corresponds to a core magical keyword. Spells are written as sequences
of rune-inscriptions that read, to a trained eye, almost like instructions: what to take,
what to shape it into, where to direct it, what to do with what remains.

The Pandor scholars describe it as the oldest written language in the world, which is
probably true. The system — the Class system — appears to have been built in Runescript.
The dead gods wrote in it. The Shelved used it. Some of the sealed ruins contain
Runescript inscriptions so long and so tightly composed that no one alive has parsed
them fully. The Pale City's archive has catalogued seventeen distinct historical dialects.
None of them have broken backward compatibility. A rune carved three thousand years ago
means what it meant then, which is either reassuring or unsettling depending on who you ask.

#### Syntax

Runescript is a functional language with COBOL-like verbosity. This is not a contradiction:
COBOL's design philosophy was that code should be readable by anyone who speaks English,
not just programmers. Runescript's design philosophy — whoever designed it — was that a
spell inscription should be readable by anyone who knows the runes, not just a [Mage].

A Runescript spell is a function: it takes Myst in, operates on it, and returns either
an effect or transformed Myst. Every spell must account for where all Myst goes.
Myst that enters a spell must either produce an effect, be stored, or be released.
A spell with no `GROUND` or `STORE` at the end is considered malformed — the Myst goes
somewhere, but the inscription does not control where.

**Basic structure:**

```
[VERB-RUNE] [NOUN or QUALIFIER chain]
  [modifier clause]
  [modifier clause]
[NEXT-VERB-RUNE] ...
```

Each line is a rune followed by its arguments. Arguments are either noun-runes, other
rune-keywords, or named values (quantities, targets, durations). Indented lines are
modifiers of the line above. A spell reads top-to-bottom, executes left-to-right within
each line.

**The pipe:** Runescript uses `INTO` as its composition operator — the output of one
rune-expression feeds as input to the next. This is the functional core of the language:
spells are pipelines.

```
ᚠ MYST FROM SELF
  INTO ᚲ REVEAL-AT TARGET
  INTO ᛊ COMPLETE-WHEN TRUTH-VISIBLE
ᛟ GROUND REMAINDER
```

This reads: "Take Myst from self. Pass it into a Reveal directed at the target. Complete
when truth is visible. Ground whatever remains."

**Named spells (functions):**

A named spell is defined once and called by name from other spells. This is Runescript's
functional composition — complex magic built from verified smaller pieces.

```
SPELL HEARTH-SEAL TAKES (KEEPER, BOUNDARY)
  ᛚ MYST FROM AMBIENT NEAR BOUNDARY
    ᚾ CONSTRAINED-TO BOUNDARY-OF KEEPER
    ᛁ STILL HOSTILE-INTENT WITHIN BOUNDARY
    ᚹ BIND STILL-CONDITION TO BOUNDARY-EDGE
    ᛃ CYCLE UNTIL KEEPER-ABSENT OR BOUNDARY-BROKEN
  ᛟ GROUND ON CYCLE-END
END HEARTH-SEAL
```

Any inscription can then call `PERFORM HEARTH-SEAL WITH (THIS-KEEPER, THIS-INN)`.
This is how the Sanctuary Skill works mechanically — an [Innkeeper] with the Skill
has the HEARTH-SEAL function loaded. When she means it, the spell executes.

**Conditionals:**

```
WHEN [condition] THEN [rune-expression]
OTHERWISE [rune-expression]
```

Conditionals read like legal clauses because they function like legal clauses.
A conditional in Runescript is a binding commitment: if the condition is true, the
expression executes; if not, the otherwise branch executes; the spell cannot do both.

**Cost declaration:**

Every spell that takes from the caster rather than ambient Myst must declare this:

```
COSTS SELF ᚢ FORCE OF MAGNITUDE [quantity]
```

A spell without a cost declaration can only draw from ambient or stored Myst.
A spell with an undeclared cost that attempts to draw from the caster anyway is
malformed — and malformed spells produce unpredictable results.

#### Example inscriptions

**A basic healing spell** — carved on a [Healer]'s practice-stone:

```
ᚠ MYST FROM AMBIENT
  INTO ᛒ GROW-TOWARD WHOLENESS IN TARGET-WOUND
  INTO ᚢ FORCE OF MAGNITUDE EQUAL-TO WOUND-DEPTH
  ᚾ CONSTRAINED-BY OWN-RESERVE NOT-EXCEEDING HALF
ᛊ COMPLETE-WHEN WOUND-CLOSES
ᛟ GROUND REMAINDER
```

A scholar reading this inscription knows: it draws from ambient, not the caster's
reserves; it scales its force to the wound's depth; it stops when the wound closes;
unused Myst is grounded. This is a safe spell. The CONSTRAIN clause prevents it from
healing more than it should. The GROUND at the end is responsible.

**A ward** — carved into an inn's door-frame:

```
SPELL INN-WARD TAKES (KEEPER, THRESHOLD)
  ᛚ MYST FROM KEEPER-INTENT
    ᛉ WARD THRESHOLD AGAINST HOSTILE-WILL
    ᚹ BIND WARD TO THRESHOLD-STONE
    ᛖ LINK WARD-STRENGTH TO KEEPER-PRESENCE
    ᛃ CYCLE WHILE KEEPER-WITHIN-WALLS
  WHEN KEEPER-ABSENT THEN ᛁ STILL WARD AT LAST-STATE
  ᛟ GROUND ON KEEPER-DEPARTURE AFTER STILL-PERIOD
END INN-WARD
```

This spell sources its Myst from the keeper's intent, not ambient. It links the ward's
strength to the keeper's presence. When she leaves, the ward goes still at its last
state — it doesn't drop immediately — and grounds after a period. The STILL clause is
why a true inn's protection persists briefly after an innkeeper leaves the room.

**A truth-binding** — carved on Deepwalker seal rings:

```
SPELL KEEPER-SEAL TAKES (STATEMENT, WITNESS, PARTIES)
  ᛚ MYST FROM WITNESS-INTENT
    ᛏ COMPEL STATEMENT AS TRUE-AT-TIME-OF-SPEAKING
    ᚷ EXCHANGE RECOGNITION BETWEEN PARTIES
    ᚹ BIND EXCHANGE TO WITNESS-MEMORY
    ᛁ STILL BINDING IN WITNESS-MEMORY UNTIL RELEASED
  WHEN STATEMENT-VIOLATED THEN
    ᛈ UNKNOWN REVEAL VIOLATION-TO WITNESS
  ᛊ COMPLETE-WHEN ALL-PARTIES ACKNOWLEDGE
ᛟ GROUND
END KEEPER-SEAL
```

This is the spell behind Deepwalker legal memory. The `STILL IN WITNESS-MEMORY` clause
is why the binding persists after the negotiation. The `REVEAL violation-to` clause is
why Deepwalker [Keepers] know when a treaty has been broken before anyone tells them.

**An improvised strike** — spoken, not carved; abbreviated and imprecise:

```
ᚦ ᚢ ᚠ
```

STRIKE. FORCE. FLOW. Three rune-words, spoken in sequence. No qualifiers, no CONSTRAIN,
no GROUND. The Myst takes the path of least resistance. This is why improvised magic is
unreliable — not because the runes don't work, but because without the full inscription
the Myst makes its own decisions about magnitude and direction. Sometimes it works.
Sometimes it works on the wrong thing.

#### What the Dry does to Runescript

The Dry is, among other things, a corruption of Runescript logic.

A healthy spell has a clear FLOW path, declared costs, and a GROUND at the end.
A Dry-corrupted inscription has specific, diagnosable failures that a trained [Scholar]
can read:

**Missing GROUND** — the most common early sign. The spell works, but Myst accumulates
rather than completing its cycle. Eventually the inscription runs hotter and hotter until
it either burns out or produces effects beyond what the inscription specifies.

**Self-referential CYCLE** — a loop condition that references itself:
`ᛃ CYCLE UNTIL CYCLE-COMPLETE`. The spell never terminates. Myst feeds into it
indefinitely. The surrounding area hollows out.

**Inverted GROW** — `ᛒ GROW-TOWARD VOID` rather than WHOLENESS. The rune is present,
the syntax is correct, but the argument is wrong. A [Healer] whose GROW has inverted
closes wounds in the wrong direction. The Skill looks like it's working. It isn't.

**COMPEL without WITNESS** — `ᛏ COMPEL STATEMENT` with no sourcing in caster or witness
intent. The compulsion draws from nowhere and from everywhere simultaneously — which in
Myst terms means it draws from the nearest available source, which is usually the target.
A truth-binding gone Dry compels the person it was meant to protect.

Scholars who study the Dry as a linguistic phenomenon note that the corruption is not
random. It makes changes that are internally consistent — wrong, but coherent.
Someone or something is editing the code. The edits have intent.
No one has confirmed what the intent is.

---

#### Who uses it and how

**The Pandor** use Runescript the way engineers use documentation — comprehensively,
carefully, with an extensive tradition of commenting and versioning. A Pandor spell
inscription includes the author, the date, the revision history, and the intended
execution context. Their archive contains spells written by people who died four
centuries ago, still executable, still accurate.

**The Lövfolk** use it in living wood — growing the runes into the tree rather than
carving them, which produces an inscription that changes as the tree grows. A Lövfolk
ward is therefore never static; it is always being amended by the tree it is written in.
This makes their magic unusually adaptive and nearly impossible to fully parse from
outside.

**The Bergfolk** engrave it into metal and stone as part of the crafting process.
A runed tool is not decorated — the runes are functional, specifying what the tool
should do when it operates. A [Runesmith]'s work is literally programming the tool.
The runes on a Bergfolk hammer specify how the force should transfer through the material
on impact. The runes on a forge specify the heat distribution. The runes on a mana-granite
conducting block specify the Myst channel geometry.

**The Everstill** study it historically. Vitstad's archive contains the largest collection
of annotated Runescript anywhere in Mistheim — working spells alongside academic analyses
alongside failed attempts alongside spells in dialects that no living scholar recognises.
The oldest inscriptions in the collection produce effects their annotations do not predict,
which the Everstill [Scholars] consider interesting rather than alarming.

**The Fae** predate Runescript, and it shows. Fae use of the language is eccentric —
syntactically correct but deliberately operating at the edges of what the specification
allows. A fae-written inscription follows all the rules and produces results that feel
like they should have been prevented by the rules. They understand the language the way
someone understands a contract when they also wrote the contract.

**The Goblins** use abbreviated oral forms — three-rune spoken sequences, like improvised
strikes — because nobody ever taught them the full system. Some goblin [Keepers] have
self-taught from salvaged inscriptions and produce idiosyncratic but functional Runescript
that would horrify a Pandor [Archivist] stylistically while working correctly. There are
more self-taught goblin [Runesmiths] than anyone has counted.

**The Bergfolk** are the world's foremost Runescript practitioners. Their hold-culture
has treated the runes as the only serious magical tradition for eight centuries. A Bergfolk
craftsperson who uses Rasa (see below) is considered to be doing something sentimental,
not something magical. This is not universally fair but is culturally consistent.

**Ordinary people** largely do not use Runescript at all. They use Rasa instead — the
older, more intuitive tradition described in the next section. The few phrases most people
know in Runescript (`ᛉ ᚹ ᛟ` — WARD BIND GROUND, spoken while locking a door) are
survival-cultural holdovers, the way people knock on wood. It does something. Scholars
disagree about how much.

---

### Rasa — the language of living feeling

If Runescript is code, Rasa is poetry that works.

Rasa is the older tradition — older than the Pandor records, older than the Bergfolk
holds, possibly older than the Class system. Most peoples in Mistheim use it naturally
and have no word for what they are doing when they do it. A [Healer] who closes a wound
by genuinely wanting it closed is using Rasa. A [Bard] who changes a room with a story
is using Rasa. An innkeeper who means the welcome — not performs it, means it — is using
Rasa. The world bends toward genuine feeling. Rasa is the formal articulation of why.

The name comes from the Javanese concept: rasa means both *feeling* and *the innermost
essence of a thing*. It is used in wayang, in keris-craft, in the mystical traditions
of the old archipelago — and in every case it refers to the same thing: the inner state
that makes an outer action true. A puppet in the hands of a dalang who merely moves it
is a puppet. In the hands of one who feels the character, it is something else. This is
Rasa. The word crossed into Mistheim through the Called and stayed because it named
something the world already understood.

---

#### The script — Aksara Rasa

Written Rasa uses a script derived from High Javanese — the Hanacaraka alphabet,
a flowing abugida in which consonants carry inherent vowels modified by diacritical
marks above and below the main character. It looks nothing like Runescript. Where
Elder Futhark runes are angular and carved — built for stone and metal — Aksara Rasa
is rounded, cursive, written with ink on paper or fabric or skin. It is a brushwork
script. The curves flow continuously; lifting the brush between characters is considered
poor form.

The characters: ꦲꦤꦕꦫꦏ ꦣꦠꦱꦮꦭ ꦥꦝꦗꦪꦚ ꦩꦒꦧꦛꦔ

Above and below each base character, small diacritical marks specify emotional tone —
not the emotion itself (that comes from the caster) but the emotional direction.
The mark for *longing* changes how the base character reads. The mark for *resolve*
changes it differently. A single character in Aksara Rasa carries more tonal information
than a full line of Runescript — and correspondingly less precision.

Most people who use Rasa do not write it at all. They speak it, or sing it, or think it
in the moment. The written form exists for teaching and for archiving — Pandor
[Chroniclers] have preserved libraries of effective Rasa phrasings, the way a collection
of poems is preserved. Reading them produces partial effects. The original caster's
state cannot be reproduced from the text alone.

---

#### How it works

A Rasa spell has three parts, spoken or felt in sequence:

**The Truth** — a statement of genuine emotional fact for the caster in this moment.
Not what they wish were true. What is true. "I am afraid of losing this person." "I am
angrier than I have ever been." "I have been in this place before and I know it can be
safe." The Truth is not a declaration — it is an acknowledgment. A caster who cannot find
the Truth cannot cast.

**The Image** — the spell's intended effect, expressed as metaphor or feeling rather than
instruction. Not "heat this room" but "I remember warmth and it was enough." Not "strike
my enemy" but "my certainty moves outward." The Image must connect emotionally to the
Truth — the feeling must want to become the effect. A disconnected Image produces nothing
or worse.

**The Release** — a single word, breath, or gesture that lets the spell leave the caster.
In Javanese tradition this is often *lunga* (go) or *dadi* (become) or simply an
exhalation with intent. In Mistheim it has a hundred forms across the peoples. The
Release is the moment the emotion becomes external.

The spell has no further instructions. It goes where the Image pointed, shaped by how
strongly the Truth was held. There is no CONSTRAIN, no cost declaration, no GROUND.
The Myst uses the caster's emotional state as its specification and finds its own path.

---

#### Example spells

**Healing — spoken by a [Healer] at the bedside of someone they love:**

> *Aku kehilangan dia sekali.*
> *This wound does not have to be.*
> *Lunga.*

"I lost them once. / This wound does not have to be. / Go."

The Truth is grief that is already real. The Image is a refusal of the wound's
necessity — not positive thinking, but genuine refusal. The Release is a breath.
The spell heals in proportion to how much the caster means the Image. It will not
heal as precisely as a Runescript inscription. It may heal faster. It will stop when
the emotional moment passes.

**Ward — spoken by an innkeeper before opening the doors:**

> *Ini rumah saya.*
> *Orang yang masuk hari ini aman.*
> *Tetap.*

"This is my house. / The people who enter today are safe. / Stay."

Clumsy in translation; the Javanese carries a weight of possession and responsibility
that "my house" does not fully convey. The ward holds as long as the innkeeper means it
— which for a true innkeeper is as long as the inn is open. When they stop meaning it,
the ward does not fall immediately. It fades over the hour following, which is why
travelers who arrive late sometimes find the warmth still present after an innkeeper has
gone to sleep.

**Strike — improvised combat, by a [Soldier] who is genuinely right:**

> *Kau salah.*
> *Ini.*

"You are wrong. / This."

Minimal. It works because the caster believes it completely. A [Soldier] who doubts
their cause cannot cast this effectively. A [Soldier] who is certain can.
The specificity of the target is determined by where the caster is looking and what
they mean by *this*. It is not a precise weapon. It is a certain one.

**Grief-light — improvised by a child who is lost and afraid:**

> *Ibu pernah menemukanku.*
> *Cahaya.*

"My mother found me before. / Light."

No formal training. No Aksara Rasa. A child who genuinely believes they will be found
and genuinely wants to be seen can produce soft illumination — enough to be visible,
not enough to navigate by. This is the most basic expression of Rasa: pure feeling,
minimal shaping. It is also the example Pandor scholars use when explaining why Rasa
cannot be taught exactly. The child is not following a formula. They are trusting a
memory.

---

#### Why it fails for masterwork work

Rasa spells are anchored in the emotional moment that cast them. That moment ends.

A ward placed with genuine welcome holds while the innkeeper means it and fades as that
specific feeling fades — not as a general attitude, but as a precise emotional state.
By morning it has drifted. A week later, the ward must be renewed or recast. This is not
a flaw in the magic — it is what the magic is. It does not outlast the feeling because
it is the feeling.

An object enchanted with Rasa holds the emotional imprint of its creation. A sword
enchanted in "my courage in this battle" carries that specific courage — which is
vivid for a season and thin by the following year, because memory loses precision.
The sword still works. It works less. By the time the event that defined it is two
generations past, the enchantment has become ambient warmth with no specific function.

Runescript enchantments do not have this problem. `ᚢ FORCE CONSTANT INTO ᚦ STRIKE`
does not depend on who carved it or what they felt. It does what it specifies, permanently,
until the stone cracks.

This is why high-level crafting uses Runescript. A masterwork blade is Runescript. A
bridge that must hold for a century is Runescript. A contract that cannot be misremembered
is Runescript. Rasa is used in the crafting process — the Bergfolk smith who enters
the right emotional state before carving makes better inscriptions — but the enchantment
itself is Rune-specified.

The reverse is also true: Rasa in the moment beats Runescript completely. A carved ward
takes days to inscribe correctly and requires expertise. A genuine innkeeper in the right
emotional state can ward an inn faster than she can lock the door. A [Healer] who means
it heals before a [Runesmith] finishes defining the wound parameters.

---

#### Who uses Rasa

Most peoples. Almost everyone.

Rasa requires no training to access — only genuine feeling and the willingness to direct
it. Children use it accidentally. Ordinary people use it without knowing it has a name.
The difference between an untrained Rasa caster and a trained one is not access but
range, precision, and sustainability. A trained [Bard] can hold the Truth and the Image
simultaneously for the length of a song. An untrained person can hold them for one breath.

**The Lövfolk** are Rasa practitioners of the first order. Their long lives give them
access to emotional depths — grief that has had three hundred years to settle, love that
has been maintained and tested and maintained again. A Lövfolk [Healer] does not simply
want the patient to heal. They have wanted patients to heal for two centuries and know
exactly what that wanting feels like. This is why Lövfolk Rasa is quiet and very effective.

**The Viddfolk** use Rasa in oral form embedded in their route-songs. The emotional
discipline required to memorise complex routes and simultaneously sustain the feeling
behind each phrase is significant. A Viddfolk [Herald] running the ley-lines is not just
following a path — they are feeling-toward the destination while reciting the route.
The ley-line responds to the sustained emotional intent.

**The Deepwalkers** use Rasa as the emotional layer beneath their oral legal memory.
The words of a treaty are Rasa-anchored: a Deepwalker [Keeper] who remembers a contract
does not merely recite it, they feel the weight of the obligation as it was felt at
signing. This is why Deepwalker legal memory is more than record-keeping. It carries
the emotional truth of the agreement.

**The Markfolk** use Rasa for agriculture — which sounds surprising until you watch a
Level-30 [Watermaster] stand at a river junction, feel for where the water wants to go,
and redirect a tributary with a single spoken phrase. She has been doing this for forty
years. She knows what it feels like when the river is wrong and what it feels like when
it is right. The [Watermaster] Class is significantly Rasa-based; the Markfolk do not
have a word for this because it has never occurred to them that it would be otherwise.

**[Bards]** are the most studied Rasa practitioners, in the sense that they have
theorised it most extensively. Bard theory is largely emotional mechanics: what kinds
of truth support what kinds of image, how to sustain a complex spell across a
performance, how to cast collectively (an audience's collective emotional state is usable
as Rasa source — this is why a [Bard]'s performance magic is stronger with a bigger
crowd that is fully present). The highest-level [Bard] Skills appear to use Runescript
phrases embedded in the story structure — the Rasa carries the spell, the Runescript
guides it.

**The Bergfolk** use Rasa less than any other people, and the ones who use it well tend
not to discuss it with other Bergfolk. Their cultural framework treats Runescript as
rigorous and Rasa as imprecise. This is technically accurate and misses the point in the
same way that calling a river imprecise compared to a pipe misses the point of rivers.
A Bergfolk master [Runesmith] who quietly uses Rasa to find the right emotional state
before carving produces work that is measurably superior to the work of those who do not.
Most of them know this and none of them want to discuss it.

**The Fae** use Rasa almost exclusively. Their magic is personal and tied to their
emotional state and accumulated knowledge of what it feels like to be a particular age
with particular experiences. Fae Rasa is exceptionally powerful — deep, specific, backed
by a Truth that is thousands of years old — and exceptionally unanalysable, because you
cannot reproduce a fae's Truth from outside. Pandor scholars have attempted to document
fae Rasa and produced descriptions that are accurate and useless, like describing the
color of something that only exists in a dream.

**The Goblins** use Rasa constantly, practically, without theory. A goblin band's oral
tradition includes hundreds of functional Rasa phrases that have been field-tested and
refined across generations — not written in Aksara Rasa, just remembered. The phrases
work because goblin communities sustain the emotional context that makes them true.
A phrase about finding value in discarded things works because the band has always found
value in discarded things and genuinely believes it. The Compact's [Keepers] are aware
that some of their most reliable field magic is Rasa-based and have simply accepted this.

---

#### Rasa and the Dry

The Dry thins Myst. Rasa is partially resistant to this — it draws from emotional truth
rather than ambient flow, and emotional truth is not immediately depleted by the Dry.

But the Dry is patient. It does not need to deplete the Myst to compromise Rasa.
It compromises the feelings instead.

A person in a Dry-affected area finds their emotional range narrowing. Not dramatically —
not a sudden numbness — but a slow loss of certainty. The [Healer] who has always known
what it felt like to want someone to heal finds herself performing the gesture without
being sure she means it. The [Bard] whose story always moved them finds it functional but
hollow. The innkeeper who means the welcome finds herself saying the words.

The spells still partially work. The Rasa finds the feeling that is there and uses what
is available. The results are thinner. A ward that would hold a week holds a day. A
healing that would close cleanly closes slowly. The quality degrades in ways that are
easy to attribute to other causes — fatigue, distraction, off days.

This is the Dry's most effective tool against Rasa. It does not break the magic. It
makes the caster uncertain whether the magic broke or they did.

Runescript does not have this vulnerability. A carved inscription does not need to feel
anything. In Dry-affected areas, Runescript continues to function until the Myst itself
falls below operational threshold — which is much further along than where Rasa begins
to fail. This is one reason the Bergfolk's position is stronger than it appears in a
world where the Dry is advancing.

---

#### The two traditions together

Runescript and Rasa are not competing schools. They are two descriptions of the same
underlying physics, approached from opposite directions.

Runescript says: specify the operation precisely, and the Myst will execute it.
Rasa says: feel the outcome genuinely, and the Myst will find a path.

Both are correct. The highest magic in Mistheim uses both: Rasa to identify and sustain
the genuine emotional intent, Runescript to shape and specify and ground the execution.
A master [Runesmith] in emotional alignment produces inscriptions that the Myst treats
as partially pre-executed. A [Bard] with Runescript phrases embedded in their story
produces effects that are both fast and durable.

The Pandor [Myst-Readers] who study both traditions describe them as two grammars for
the same language. Runescript is explicit grammar — every rule written out. Rasa is
implicit grammar — learned through immersion, felt rather than stated. Fluent speakers
of both can move between them mid-sentence.

The Shelved, when they have been observed using magic, appear to do this. No one has
been able to determine which tradition came first.

---

Vattenpandalandet's peoples are not divided into heroes and monsters. The beings the world
calls monsters are the ones whose complexity it cannot afford to acknowledge.

**The Deepwalkers** — humanoid peoples of the coasts and deep places, clannish, with a
tradition of oral memory so precise it functions as a legal system. Their histories go back
further than any written record. They know things about the world's previous ages that the
land peoples do not. Corruption transforms them into territorial and aggressive versions of
themselves — but underneath, the Deepwalker is still there, still remembering.

**The Shelved** — beings of enormous age and uncertain form who sleep in the ruins of previous
civilisations. Not hostile by default. Do not respond well to being woken. Some of them built
the system. None of them will say so.

**The Wandering People** — a collective noun for any being who travels between regions without
fixed territory. This includes itinerant [Bards], [Merchants], [Scholars], exiles, and outcasts
of every species. Inns exist to serve them. The Wandering People carry information across the
world in ways that no institution controls, which makes them valuable and dangerous.

**The Called** — people from elsewhere who arrived in Vattenpandalandet and did not leave.
Some fell from the sky. Some walked out of ruins that should have been empty. Some simply
appeared. They carry knowledge from places the world does not have words for. They adapt,
gain Classes, build lives. They are not always human. The system seems to accommodate anything.

**Goblins** — present across the continent in bands, settlements, and occasionally whole
cities, though none of the histories written by larger peoples document them accurately.
They have Classes. A [Goblin Chef] and a [Human Chef] learn from the same system;
the system makes no distinction between them. This is not widely acknowledged by
cultures that have built their self-image around goblin exterminability.

They are small, fast, and deeply social. A goblin band is not a mob — it is a
community, with internal hierarchy, long memory, and a sense of humour that skews dark
given their circumstances. They do not forget an inn that served them without comment.
They do not forget the village that burned their settlement either. Their [Bards] and
[Keepers] carry oral histories of things the written record deliberately omitted.

The world calls them monsters because it is cheaper than calling them people. The system
disagrees, quietly and without comment, by continuing to give them Skills regardless.
When a goblin band shelters in a true inn, the inn's protection extends to them. The
innkeeper's Skills do not ask for documentation of personhood.

Corruption affects goblin bands the same way it affects every community: it finds what is
already fragile. A band that has lost too many and grown too desperate is vulnerable. But
so is a settled people that has spent generations treating them as obstacles rather than
neighbours. Corruption does not distinguish between those two failures.

### Inns

An inn in Vattenpandalandet is a sanctuary. This is not metaphor — it is law older than the
current kingdoms. An innkeeper who keeps a true inn, who means it, gains Skills that enforce
this: violence does not happen here, cold does not reach inside, strangers are briefly safe from
the things that pursue them.

Inns are where people who should never meet share a table. A Deepwalker and an exile and a
wandering [Bard] and something that arrived from elsewhere — all eating something warm, all
briefly equal. This is the inn's political act. Corruption hates this. It targets innkeepers
early and specifically, because the alternative — people seeing each other as people — is the
one thing that reliably slows it.

### Stories and art

Stories are civilisation-building in Vattenpandalandet. The [Bard] class carries the same
cultural weight as [General] or [King]. A story told well can change a city's politics.
A [Bard] who is genuinely skilled can activate Skills that rewrite how people in a room
perceive each other — not through illusion but through emotional truth. This is treated as
real and slightly frightening even by the people who admire it.

The world has accumulated so many stories over so many ages that some of them have become
load-bearing. Old tales about the nature of the corruption, the origin of the system, the
first [Innkeeper] — these are not just mythology. They describe things that are still true.
Corruption rewrites stories. It does not destroy them — it introduces false endings, changes
what the hero chose, makes the monster's victory seem inevitable. A corrupted [Bard] does not
lose their voice. They lose their ability to tell a story that ends well.

### The weight of the past

There were dead gods. Their names are not said aloud. Their remains are in the ground, in the
stone, in the system itself — because the system was made in the age when gods were alive and
they built things that outlasted them.

There were previous ages. Each one thought it was the last and most sophisticated. Each one
ended. What ended them is not agreed upon and possibly also not fully gone.

The ancient ruins of Vattenpandalandet are not set dressing. They are questions that the
world cannot answer but cannot stop asking. Dungeoneers enter them for Skills and treasure and
come out having seen something they are not able to describe. Some do not come out.

### Writing Vattenpandalandet lore

The prose voice here is warmer and more oral than Earth's. Write as if you are a storyteller
who has heard this story from someone who heard it from someone else — and each telling adds
something. Notice small things: what something smells like, what someone eats, how the light
falls at dusk. Let warmth and humour exist even inside dark entries — the world earns its
tragedy because it also earns its joy.

Species described as monsters should have interiority. If you are writing about a creature
that the world exterminates on sight, give it a meal it prefers, a sound it makes when it
is not afraid, something it remembers.

Do not explain the system. The system is real and present and characters live inside it —
they do not lecture about it. A [Blacksmith] levelling does not monologue about Class
mechanics. She just feels something shift and knows she is better at reading the metal.

**Give characters a story before a power.** People in Vattenpandalandet think about the debt
they owe, the person they cannot forgive, the inn they are trying to reach before dark, the
argument they have been having for fifteen years. Their Class and their relationship to Myst
are real — but they are not who the character is. They are what the character became.

A Mistheim scholar is a scholar first. They have a favourite text. They have a home settlement
that may or may not exist anymore. Their Class is [Chronicler] or [Cartographer] or [Keeper].
When writing any Mistheim character: begin with what they remember and what they have lost.
The rest follows.

## Nations, peoples, and the world between them

This section is the primary reference for lore agents writing about Mistheim's peoples,
political geography, and culture. It supersedes generic fantasy assumptions. Every people
listed here is specific to this world — not a fantasy trope wearing a name-tag.

### The geography in brief

Mistheim is a continent-sized landmass with a northern mountain spine, eastern ancient
forests, a central highland region of bamboo and mist valleys, southern river plains, and
a deeply indented coastline that creates hundreds of sheltered harbours. The terrain was
shaped partly by Myst: ley-lines run along the old river valleys like veins, pooling in
certain highland hollows, thinning in the high passes and the open steppe. Where Myst
pools, settlements grew. Where it thins, only the hardiest peoples go.

The previous ages left ruins everywhere — buried under farmland, half-submerged in coastal
shallows, entire cities sealed inside the northern mountains. The world is not built on top
of its history. It is built around it, beside it, occasionally inside it.

---

### The Bergfolk — dwarves of the northern holds

**Where they live:** The northern mountain spine, in a network of underground cities
connected by carved passes and trade-roads. The Bergfolk call their nation the Dyprike —
the Deep Kingdom — though it is less a kingdom than a confederation of independent holds
that agree not to fight each other more than twice a generation.

**What they make:** Everything that requires metal, stone, or patience. The Bergfolk are
the world's foremost smiths. Not because dwarves are born with special aptitude — they are
not — but because their underground cities have been refining these crafts continuously for
eight centuries, and the knowledge has compounded. A Level 20 [Runesmith] in the Dyprike
has access to techniques that nowhere else has rediscovered.

Their second great industry is Myst-conducting stone. Certain granite formations in the
northern mountains are naturally threaded with crystallised Myst — the Bergfolk mine it,
cut it into channeling blocks, and sell it to anyone who builds with magic. Half the
enchanted infrastructure in Mistheim sits on Bergfolk foundations.

**Resources:** Deep iron, copper, tin, mana-granite, underground fungi (staple food),
geothermal heat (energy for smelting). Scarce: agricultural land, timber, open sky.

**What shapes them:** Bergfolk measure status by what you have made, not who your parents
were. A Level 2 [Laborer] who inherited a hold seat is looked down on. A first-generation
[Runesmith] who levelled through forty years of craft is not. This creates a culture that
values visible skill and distrusts abstraction — the Pandor scholars make them uncomfortable,
not because they disrespect learning, but because scholarship produces nothing you can hold.

Representative Classes: [Runesmith], [Stoneshaper], [Deepminer], [Brewmaster], [Forgekeeper].

**Relations:**
- *Markfolk:* Trade is essential — ore for food — but old disputes over the southern
  foothills (where Markfolk farms meet Bergfolk mining claims) never fully resolve.
- *Lövfolk:* Cold. The elves want the eastern hardwood preserved; the Bergfolk want it
  for charcoal and construction. The disagreement is centuries old and neither side has
  moved.
- *Steinfolk:* The closest thing to genuine allies. They build; the Bergfolk supply
  what they build with. Mutual respect between craft traditions.
- *Goblins:* The Bergfolk maintain that goblins don't exist in their territory. This is
  false. The goblin salvage communities in the abandoned upper levels of three old holds
  are so useful (they clear debris, map collapses, and work cheap) that nobody with
  authority has investigated.

**The Dry's effect:** The mana-granite deposits are producing less. The crystallised Myst
in the stone is still there, but it channels less cleanly — tools built on Bergfolk
foundations are losing a fraction of their enchantment. Demand for the stone hasn't
dropped yet because nobody knows why the quality is declining. The Bergfolk don't
know either.

---

### The Lövfolk — elves of the eastern canopy

**Where they live:** The eastern ancient forests — not all of them, and not uniformly.
The Lövfolk city-states sit in the high canopy, connected by rope-road networks strung
between trees that are older than any Lövfolk alive. The nation is called the Grenmark,
but it is less a nation than a loose association of city-states that share a legal
tradition and a deep mutual distrust of anything from outside the treeline.

**What they make:** The Lövfolk have two craft exports: enchanted woodwork and medicinal
herbalism. Grenmark bows are accurate beyond what physics should allow. Grenmark herbal
compounds treat conditions other healers have no name for. Both industries depend on
Myst-dense living wood and on botanical knowledge accumulated across lifetimes measured
in centuries.

Their third, less-acknowledged export is memory. The oldest trees in the eastern forests
function as living archives — Myst has saturated them long enough that they retain
impressions of events. A [Keeper] who knows how to read a tree can extract historical
records that no written document survived. The Lövfolk maintain this as a closely held
skill and use it as diplomatic currency.

**Resources:** Myst-dense hardwood (enchanting), medicinal plants, living archives (old
trees), forest game. Scarce: metal, stone, open farmland.

**What shapes them:** Slow ageing is not the gift outsiders imagine. After 300 years,
a Lövfolk has developed opinions so calcified that changing them requires an event of
genuine seismic scale. The Grenmark city-states are politically paralysed in ways their
leaders cannot see — the same governing councils have held the same positions for four
centuries, and their policies describe a world that no longer exists. Young Lövfolk
(anyone under 80) are restless and frequently leave. The ones who stay and eventually
gain influence tend to arrive at the same positions as the generation before them, for
reasons they would not recognise as repetition.

The Lövfolk who leave are disproportionately represented among the Wandering People,
among the Compact of Knowing's scholars, and among the Called. They leave because they
are tired of waiting for the world to fit the shape their elders remember.

Representative Classes: [Keeper], [Leafweaver], [Bowwright], [Herbalist], [Canopy Scout].

**Relations:**
- *Pandor:* The relationship that functions. The Pandor need access to the living
  archives; the Lövfolk need neutral ground for their internal disputes. Both sides
  maintain a [Scholar]'s courtesy that papers over the deeper tensions.
- *Bergfolk:* See above. The hardwood dispute is the public conflict. The private one
  is that the Bergfolk's mana-granite channels are disrupting the Myst flow in the
  forest's western edge and nobody has admitted this officially.
- *Fae:* Complicated. Fae courts exist under several Lövfolk groves. The Lövfolk
  didn't invite them; the fae were there before the trees were saplings. Polite
  coexistence, built on the mutual understanding that neither side can make the
  other leave.

**The Dry's effect:** The living archives are going quiet. Trees that have been
accumulating memory for millennia are no longer adding to their record. Healers
whose Skills require Myst-attuned plants are finding the compounds less potent. The
Grenmark councils have not acknowledged this publicly. Several younger [Keepers]
have begun independently contacting the Pandor.

### The Markfolk — orcs of the southern plains

**Where they live:** The southern river delta and plains — the most agriculturally
productive land on the continent. The Markfolk nation is called the Merkförbund, and
it is genuinely a confederation: forty-three settled cities and two hundred smaller
farming communities, governed by a rotating council that meets in a different city
each year. It is chaotic in exactly the way that a confederation of farmers who
all have strong opinions about irrigation is chaotic.

**What they make:** Food. The Merkförbund feeds a significant fraction of the
continent. Their river management is the best in the world — the Steinfolk built
the infrastructure, but the Markfolk [Watermasters] run it, and the system reflects
centuries of refinement by people who live and die by its performance. Their
secondary export is clay pottery and fired goods, which they produce at scale and
trade at prices no other region can match.

Markfolk [Farmer] and [Watermaster] Classes reach levels that other nations find
embarrassing to acknowledge, given how little cultural respect they assign to farming.
A Level 35 [Watermaster] who manages a river tributary is, by the system's accounting,
more powerful than many a [Knight]. The water listens to her. The Lövfolk, who employ
Level 15 [Herbalists] in positions of cultural prestige, do not discuss this.

**Resources:** Fertile farmland, river systems, clay, livestock, flood-plain sediment
(exceptional soil). Scarce: metal, stone, deep timber.

**What shapes them:** The Markfolk have spent centuries being regarded as unsophisticated
by peoples who eat their grain and sell them metal at markup. The cultural response to
this is not bitterness exactly — it is a very dry awareness of exactly how dependent
everyone is on them, which they keep to themselves except in the company of other
Markfolk, where it becomes the basis of a very dry comedy tradition.

They have a complicated relationship with goblins. Officially, the Merkförbund outlaws
goblin settlement within its borders. In practice, the marginal farming communities on the
confederation's eastern edge quietly employ goblin [Salvagers] who clear ruins, map
flood drainage, and work without asking questions about their legal status. The farmers
don't start this conversation. Neither do the goblins.

Representative Classes: [Watermaster], [Farmer], [Herd-caller], [Riverkeeper],
[Clay-shaper].

**Relations:**
- *Bergfolk:* Essential trade, manageable tension. The foothills dispute flares and
  cools on a roughly twenty-year cycle.
- *Steinfolk:* Genuine respect. The Steinfolk built the river infrastructure; the
  Markfolk run it; both sides understand the dependency runs both ways.
- *Centaurs (Viddfolk):* Grazing rights disputes on the eastern steppe border. Long
  history. The Viddfolk need the steppe for their route-claims; the Merkförbund wants
  to extend farming into it. No war, but the negotiation has been ongoing for sixty
  years and no one expects it to conclude.

**The Dry's effect:** Myst-touched river water has been making crops more fertile
for three centuries. The effect is so integrated into Markfolk farming practice that
most farmers cannot identify where their methods end and the Myst begins. The yields
are still strong. The trend is not visible yet. The [Watermasters] who monitor river
Myst flow have noticed. They have not yet told the council.

### The Viddfolk — centaurs of the open steppe

**Where they live:** Everywhere, technically. The Viddfolk do not have a capital.
They have three Speaking-Grounds — established neutral meeting places in the open steppe
where their council gathers every five years — and beyond that, they have route-claims:
stretches of road, path, and open ground that the other nations have agreed to recognise
as Viddfolk jurisdiction.

The agreement that created this system is called the Vidde Accords. It is the oldest
continually observed treaty in Mistheim. The reason every nation observes it is that
everyone needs the Viddfolk, and everyone knows it.

**What they make:** Nothing that can be warehoused. The Viddfolk run Mistheim's only
reliable long-distance communication network. Their [Heralds] carry messages, contracts,
and diplomatic correspondence between nations at speeds that no mounted rider matches —
not because of superior horses, but because high-level [Heralds] have learned to ride
the ley-lines. A Myst-current running along a river valley makes a Viddfolk [Herald]
move like wind.

They also make decisions. This is not something they would describe as a product, but
it functions as one. When two nations want to negotiate, they call a Viddfolk
[Herald-of-Holding], who facilitates. The system works because the Viddfolk have
no territorial interest in the outcome and have demonstrated, over centuries, that they
will carry a message accurately even between parties that are actively at war. They
do not take sides. They take the road.

**Resources:** Nothing they own; everything they pass through. The steppe provides grazing.
The ley-lines provide speed. Their real resource is trust, and they manage it like capital.

**What shapes them:** The Viddfolk do not build things. This is a philosophical position
as much as a practical one — they believe that anything worth having should be able to be
carried, remembered, or outrun. They have an oral tradition of extraordinary complexity:
maps memorised as songs, treaty terms embedded in stories, genealogies of every major
family in every nation they deal with. A Level-20 [Herald] knows more secrets about the
continent's political arrangements than any king's advisor.

The question of who becomes a [Herald] is not inherited. It is earned. A young Viddfolk
who cannot demonstrate the memorisation standard by adulthood is not excluded from their
community, but they do not run the routes. This creates a culture in which memory is
treated as the highest skill and forgetting is treated as a moral failing in a way that
strikes outsiders as harsh.

Representative Classes: [Herald], [Route-Keeper], [Steppe Scout], [Wind-Reader].

**Relations:**
- *All nations, diplomatically:* The Viddfolk are neutral and known to be neutral.
  They will carry a message to an enemy. They will not take payment to delay it.
- *Goblins:* The Goblin Compact's shadow network and the Viddfolk share an arrangement.
  The goblins provide information from the ruins (accessible to no one else); the
  Viddfolk carry it to people who can use it, taking a percentage. Neither side has
  acknowledged this arrangement to anyone else. It is the most functional political
  relationship either party has.

**The Dry's effect:** The ley-lines are thinning. [Heralds] who used to run the major
routes in three days are taking four, five. The Viddfolk have kept this internal so far —
an admission of slowing would damage the trust their economy is built on. The route-songs
are being quietly recalculated. The new timings have not been shared with clients.

### The Steinfolk — minotaurs of the five strongholds

**Where they live:** Five major strongholds distributed across the continent — each one
built at a point where the geography presented a problem and the Steinfolk solved it.
One at the river delta junction where flooding was destroying farmland. One at the
northern mountain pass where avalanche closed the Bergfolk trade road every winter.
One at the southern coast where the harbour silted up and killed a city. One at the
forest edge where the old bridge rotted and nobody else would build a new one. One at
the centre of the continent, for reasons that the Steinfolk have never explained and
nobody has pressed them on.

The nation is called the Femhörn — the Five Horns. It has no capital. Each stronghold
is self-governing. They convene annually and make decisions that the strongholds observe
with something approaching reliability.

**What they make:** Infrastructure. The Steinfolk are the world's builders — not by
specialisation but by calling. Five centuries ago, a catastrophic series of floods
destroyed most of the lowland settlements. The Steinfolk were the ones who dredged
the rivers, constructed the sea-walls, redirected the tributaries. Other nations called
for help and the Steinfolk came. When the crisis was over, they stayed, because the
structures needed maintaining.

They have been maintaining them since. Every major river junction in Mistheim has a
Steinfolk [Engineer] or [Bridgekeeper] on site, doing the work nobody else learned
because they always assumed the Steinfolk would handle it. The Steinfolk are aware of
this. They have never made it a bargaining position. They have not decided whether
this is dignity or missed opportunity.

**Resources:** Stone, river access (which they built, and therefore understand better
than anyone). Scarce: farmland, forest.

**What shapes them:** The Steinfolk have been called to help, helped, and then
not called again until the next crisis, so many times that they have developed a dry
patience about it that reads as inscrutability to outsiders. They are not unfriendly.
They are experienced. A Steinfolk [Engineer] who has rebuilt the same flood wall four
times does not express frustration about it. She just notes, quietly, what would
prevent it being rebuilt a fifth.

Their ritual traditions are tied to what they have built. A stronghold celebrates the
anniversary of its founding structure. A craftsperson's most important ceremony is the
laying of the first stone of a project that matters. Death rites involve adding one
stone to whatever the deceased built — if nothing is left, a memorial stone is cut and
placed somewhere meaningful. The Femhörn culture is shaped by the belief that what
you leave standing is who you were.

Representative Classes: [Engineer], [Bridgekeeper], [Stoneshaper], [Flood-Warden],
[Structural Keeper].

**Relations:**
- *Markfolk:* Genuine mutual respect. The irrigation system is jointly maintained.
  The relationship is the most functional bilateral one in Mistheim.
- *Bergfolk:* Allied under the Building Alliance. The Steinfolk build; the Bergfolk
  provide materials. Occasional disagreements about construction method that are
  resolved slowly and without drama.
- *Everyone else:* The Steinfolk are where you go when something is broken. Nations
  that have been dismissive of them discover, eventually, that the Steinfolk remember
  it. Not as a grievance. As data.

### The Pandor — panda scholars of the highland mist

**Where they live:** The central highlands — bamboo ridges, mist-filled valleys, standing
stones. Their settlements are called kloster: scholarly communities that function as
archive, school, and local government simultaneously. The largest, Mistklostret, sits at
the highest permanent mist-line and has been continuously inhabited for six hundred years.

The Pandor do not have a nation in the political sense. They have the Compact of Knowing:
a treaty of scholarly neutrality observed by most of the continent's major powers, which
grants Pandor scholars safe passage, right of archive access, and formal political
immunity in exchange for their commitment to share their knowledge with anyone who asks.

**What they make:** Records. The Pandor have been writing things down since before most
of the current nations existed. They write everything: weather, trade volumes, political
decisions, the testimony of witnesses to events that nobody else thought worth documenting.
Their secondary industry is cartography — the most accurate maps of the continent's
physical geography are Pandor-made. Their tertiary industry is brewing: the kloster
produces a mist-fermented grain drink that is served at every serious negotiation in
Mistheim, which the Pandor do not discuss as leverage and everyone understands it to be.

**Resources:** Bamboo (construction, writing material), highland mineral springs (the
source of the mist-ferment), ancient ruins (the highlands have ruins from at least three
distinct previous ages, which the Pandor study and catalogue). Scarce: metal, farmland.

**What shapes them:** The Pandor believe that losing a record is a moral failure. Not a
mistake — a failure. A destroyed text, a burned archive, a scholar who died without
transmitting their work: these are treated as genuine harms in Pandor culture, on a par
with violence. This makes them exceptionally careful, occasionally obsessive, and
sometimes insufferable to anyone who needs a quick decision rather than a thorough account.

It also makes them the only party in Mistheim that all other nations trust to keep their
secrets — because the Pandor write everything down, but they also have an extensive
tradition of sealed records, time-locked archives, and information access tiers that
predate the system's existence.

Representative Classes: [Chronicler], [Cartographer], [Keeper], [Archivist], [Myst-Reader].

**The Dry's effect:** The Pandor know more about the Dry than any other nation. They have
been observing it for longer. Their records include accounts of previous thins — smaller
events in previous ages where Myst flow declined in specific regions. None of those
accounts ended well. The Pandor have not shared this information publicly. They are still
deciding what to do with it.

### The Deepwalkers — coastal peoples of the long memory

**Where they live:** The coastlines, the river mouths, the sea-facing cliffs. Deepwalker
settlements are built half on land and half over water — on stilts, on floating platforms,
on structures that can be abandoned and re-established somewhere else if the sea demands
it. They are not nomadic. They are flexible. There is a difference.

The Deepwalkers do not have a single nation. They have the Tidekin — a collective term
for a dozen coastal clan-confederations that share a legal tradition and a language
and a great deal of mutual suspicion. What unifies them is the oral memory system: every
Deepwalker [Keeper] participates in the same living archive, which is maintained across
all the clans simultaneously. A Deepwalker [Keeper] in the northern harbours and one in
the southern delta are remembering the same treaty, word-for-word, in the same form it
was first spoken.

**What they make:** Salt. Navigation. Legal services. The Deepwalker salt trade supplies
most of the continent and has for centuries. Their [Navigator] Classes produce the
finest maritime charts available — not maps, exactly, but something more like annotated
memories of how the sea moves in particular conditions. Their [Keeper] services are
employed by every major nation: a contract witnessed by a Deepwalker [Keeper] is
considered legally binding in a way that signed paper alone is not.

**Resources:** Fish, salt, sea-access, coastal salvage from sunken ruins. Scarce: metal,
stone, inland farmland.

**What shapes them:** Memory is not metaphor for the Deepwalkers. It is the thing the
system rewards most in their culture and the thing they genuinely fear losing most.
Deepwalker corruption looks like forgetting — the Dry strips their Skills first at the
point where memory intersects with Myst, and a [Keeper] whose Skills are corrupted does
not lose their voice. They lose their certainty. They begin to misremember. This is
treated as one of the worst things that can happen to a person.

Representative Classes: [Keeper], [Navigator], [Salt-Master], [Tide-Warden], [Diver].

**Relations:**
- *Merfolk:* See Merfolk entry. Complicated. Both claim authority over the same deep
  places and neither fully acknowledges the other's claim.
- *Pandor:* Strong alliance. The Compact of Knowing shares a legal framework that the
  Deepwalker [Keepers] and Pandor [Archivists] both participate in. They are the two
  peoples most actively terrified by the Dry, for related reasons.

### The Merfolk — peoples of the deep places

**Where they live:** Underwater. The specific geography is not fully mapped by any
surface nation, which is exactly how the Merfolk prefer it. They occupy the deep channels,
the sea-floor ruins, the underwater cave systems beneath the coastal cliffs. Surface
contact happens at specific tidal points — underwater entrances to half-submerged ruins,
sea-cave meeting chambers, certain harbour moorings where the water is deep enough that
Merfolk can surface without leaving their element.

They are not a unified nation. They are loosely described as the Djupvolk — a term they
use for themselves collectively, without implying governance — and their internal
politics are not accessible to surface peoples.

**What they make:** Things that come from the deep: pressure-crystal (a mineral that
forms only under deep-ocean conditions, used in high-level enchanting), Myst-kelp
compounds (a biological Myst concentrate with no surface analogue), and intelligence about
the sunken ruins. The ruins under the sea are more intact than surface ones — no weather,
no salvagers, no agricultural encroachment. The Merfolk have been living adjacent to them
for centuries and know them better than anyone.

**Resources:** Deep-sea mineral formations, Myst-kelp, intact sunken ruins, deep-ocean
Myst currents (less affected by the Dry than surface flows). Scarce: surface access,
trade relationships with land-based nations.

**What shapes them:** The Djupvolk interact with the surface on their own terms and at
their own timing. They do not respond to urgency. They respond to patience. A diplomatic
mission that cannot wait three tidal cycles for a response will not get one. This is not
obstruction. It is how Merfolk time works: they plan across decades, and a nation that
wants an answer this week has misunderstood the relationship.

Their Classes reflect deep-ocean expertise: [Depth-Keeper], [Pressure-Smith], [Current-
Reader], [Ruin-Walker]. The last class is of particular interest to the Pandor, who have
been trying to establish a formal knowledge-exchange agreement for thirty years. The
Djupvolk have not yet replied.

**The Dry's effect:** The deep Myst currents are more stable than surface flows. The
Djupvolk have noticed the surface changes without yet being directly affected, which puts
them in a position of quiet advantage: their trade goods are still at full strength while
Lövfolk enchantments are weakening. They have not commented on this.

### The Goblins — the Compact and its shadow

**Where they live:** Everywhere that larger peoples have not actively cleared them from,
and some places they have. The twenty largest goblin settlements form the Goblinsamfund —
the Goblin Compact — an informal network with no capital, no formal governance, and more
effective intelligence than most nations' spy services.

Most goblin communities exist in the margins: the ruins at a city's edge, the abandoned
levels of a Bergfolk hold, the flood-land too unstable for Markfolk farming, the sea-cave
below a Deepwalker stilt-town. This is not chosen poverty. It is the result of centuries
of being cleared out of better places. The Compact has learned to make margins work.

**What they make:** Salvage, information, and repairs. Goblin [Delvers] and [Salvagers]
have been mapping the ruins for centuries. They know the location of intact pre-age
structures that no surface nation has found, because they found them in the decades
between one civilisation leaving and the next arriving. The Compact's actual trade is
this knowledge, exchanged quietly and selectively: to the Viddfolk, who carry it; to
the Pandor, who archive it; to independent merchants who pay without asking provenance.

Their repair economy is the visible surface. Goblin [Tinkerers] and [Menders] work cheap
and ask no questions. A Markfolk farm-machine that no licensed [Engineer] will come out
to fix gets fixed by the goblin settlement three fields over. This is the relationship
in miniature: tolerated, necessary, unacknowledged.

Representative Classes: [Delver], [Salvager], [Tinkerer], [Mender], [Band-Keeper].

**The Dry's effect:** The ruins the Compact has been mapping are, in some cases, pre-Dry
infrastructure — buildings from previous ages when Myst flowed differently. The
[Delvers] are finding that some sealed ruins are opening as the Myst-locked doors
weaken. What is inside them is not always inert. The Compact has not shared this
information publicly yet. They are still deciding what it's worth.

### The Fae — the Hollow Courts

**Where they live:** The Hollow Courts exist in the liminal spaces: the edge of the
forest where it meets the field, the hour before dawn, the underneath of old bridges,
the back rooms of certain inns that have been open long enough. Fae territory is not
mappable in the conventional sense because it exists in layers over other peoples'
land. The fae have been in those places longer than the peoples who now think they own
them.

There are two courts, loosely. The Court of What Was concerns itself with memory, debt,
and the past; its adherents are drawn to old things, old places, old promises. The
Court of What May Be concerns itself with potential, possibility, and unspent Myst;
its adherents appear disproportionately in places where something is about to change.
The courts have no official relation to each other and a great deal of unofficial
commerce.

**What they make:** Deals. The fae do not produce goods. They arrange exchanges —
and the terms of a fae arrangement bind in ways that even the Class system cannot
override, which suggests the fae predate the system. What they exchange varies: a
truth for a name, a memory for a passage, a year's work for a door that leads
somewhere useful. The deals are always technically fair. The complications arise
from what "fair" means when one party has been alive for a thousand years and the
other has not.

**Resources:** Access to deep Myst concentrations that no surface people have mapped.
Accumulated knowledge of the previous ages that they do not share directly.

**What shapes them:** The fae who have gained Classes are considered eccentric by their
own courts. Most fae do not interact with the system — they operate on terms older than
it, and the system seems uncertain what to do with them. The ones who do gain Classes
tend toward [Keeper], [Bard], or [Innkeeper] — roles that involve exchange, story, and
making places safe for strangers, which the fae understand from the inside.

Inns are the one place where the fae will sit across a table from anyone. Not because
the sanctuary compels them — it doesn't, exactly — but because the inn's intent resonates
with something in their own tradition. A true innkeeper and a fae court leader recognise
each other as doing the same thing by different means.

**The Dry's effect:** The Court of What Was is disturbed. Old promises are fraying —
not breaking, but losing the binding quality they have held for centuries. Some deals
struck in previous ages are starting to behave as if the party on the other side no
longer exists. The Court of What May Be is paying very close attention to the ruins
that the Compact's [Delvers] are finding. Neither court has explained this to anyone.

### The Giants — the Seven

There are seven known giants in Mistheim. There may be more in the northern wastes and
deep ocean, but the seven who have been in contact with other peoples for long enough
to be named are: Aldriksson, Bergamora, Hjelmfall, Skörna, Vötur, Klev, and the one
called simply Oldest, who has not given a different name and no one has pressed for one.

Each has occupied their territory — a mountain, a valley, a stretch of coast — for
longer than any history records. The territories have been shaped by their presence.
Aldriksson's valley has a drainage pattern that no surveyor can explain by natural
hydrology. Bergamora's mountain has mineral formations on its eastern face that look
deliberate. Skörna's coastline has never silted, despite conditions that should have
closed the harbour centuries ago.

The giants do not form a nation. They do not confer. They have the Old Accords: an
agreement between all recognised nations not to conduct warfare in giant territories, not
to attempt to extract resources from them without negotiation, and not to wake a giant
who has gone to sleep. The accords were not made with the giants. They were made after
enough nations learned why such an agreement was necessary.

Aldriksson is the only one who speaks regularly with other peoples. He finds this mildly
amusing and does it partly for entertainment. His advice, when given, tends to be unhelpful
in the short term and correct in the long term. The other six are aware of the surface
peoples in the way that a mountain is aware of weather. They do not have Classes.
The system apparently decided this would be inappropriate and has not revisited the decision.

**The Dry's effect:** Aldriksson mentioned, once, that he had seen this before. When
asked to elaborate, he described weather patterns from three previous ages in enough
detail to cross-reference with Pandor records. The Pandor sent a delegation. Aldriksson
agreed to discuss it. He set the meeting for next spring, which is in fourteen months.

### The Dragons — the territorial powers

Five major dragon territories are currently recognised. Each is named for the dragon who
holds it and is observed by surrounding nations as roughly equivalent to a sovereign
boundary, though the dragons did not negotiate this. It simply became policy after
enough people learned the cost of ignoring it.

**Grimmvald** holds the eastern forest's southern edge — which puts his territory in
permanent contact with Lövfolk land. The working relationship is tense but functional.
He has a [Patron] Class, which no one can explain. Several young Lövfolk have
received his patronage. What this means in practice is unclear, but they come back
changed and say little about it.

**Iskuldr** holds the northern mountain spine's highest peaks — technically overlapping
with Bergfolk territory, but the Bergfolk mine the lower slopes by agreement, paying
tribute in fine tools. Iskuldr has not used any of the tools. He keeps them. This is
read as approval.

**Saltmara** holds the deep southern coast. Her territory includes three sunken ruin
sites of significant historical interest. She is aware of this. Pandor archaeological
requests have been formally submitted. She has not responded. Several unofficial Deepwalker
expeditions have gone into the area. None have returned with damage or theft; two returned
with notes indicating the ruins had been recently reorganised.

**Dvergfast** and **Rödrök** hold territories in the central highlands and the open steppe
respectively. Neither has had meaningful contact with surface peoples in living memory.
They are included in the Accords for completeness.

### The Everstill — undead of the Pale City

**Where they live:** The Pale City — Vitstad — built in the ruins of a second-age capital
that occupies a plateau in the northern foothills. It has been continuously inhabited
for four hundred years by people who died and did not stay dead, without the involvement
of corruption.

The Everstill are beings whose Classes apparently have provisions for continuation. A
[Scholar] who died mid-research woke up two days later and continued. An [Innkeeper]
who could not leave their inn stayed after death and still runs it — the Sanctuary Skill
still holds, which has been tested. A [Soldier] at his post when his nation collapsed
was never discharged and remains. They do not age. They do not eat. They do not sleep.

Vitstad is one of the largest libraries in Mistheim. A community of beings that does not
die, does not sleep, and has no need for subsistence agriculture has used its time
primarily for learning. The collection covers subjects no living nation has the patience
to maintain: detailed economic records from four previous ages, technical documents for
infrastructure that no longer exists, navigational charts for coastlines that have
changed, philosophical texts from traditions that ended with their authors.

**What they make:** Access to the archive, when they choose to grant it. Preservation
services (they are better at maintaining fragile documents than anyone living). Limited
consulting on previous-age technical problems. One [Innkeeper] whose inn is still
operating and is considered one of the most remarkable establishments in Mistheim — not
for comfort, but for the company.

**Relations:** Complicated, mainly by other peoples' discomfort. The Compact of Knowing
treats Vitstad as a partner institution. The Bergfolk find the existence of [Liches] who
have been levelling their craft Skills for two centuries industrially threatening and do
not discuss it. Most nations maintain formal diplomatic relations and informal unease.

**The Dry's effect:** The Everstill are observing something. Certain undead whose
continuation was tied to a specific Myst-anchored location have found the anchor weakening.
This is the first sign any of the Everstill have had that their condition might not be
permanent. They have not shared this publicly. Vitstad's archivists are searching the
collection for precedent.

### The Constructs — the Unmade and Remade

There are forty-three active Constructs in Mistheim that have been documented by the
Pandor. Of these, twenty-two predate the current age. The oldest is estimated at
eleven hundred years. Whether it was made by the Shelved, by a previous-age civilisation
that used similar methods, or by something else entirely is not established.

Constructs in Mistheim are legally persons in the Compact of Knowing's framework —
a Pandor classification contested by the Bergfolk, who also manufacture constructs and
are uncomfortable with the implications for their newer models. The older constructs
find this debate mildly interesting. They have been persons in the practical sense for
long enough that the legal question seems academic.

Most constructs work as [Guardians] of places nobody else wants to guard: ruins, disputed
territory, road junctions, structures that require consistent maintenance. Several have
developed into [Scholars] or [Keepers]. One, called the Librarian, has been the primary
archivist of Vitstad for two hundred years, which the Everstill treat as entirely normal
and which causes outsiders to need a moment.

The constructs made by the Bergfolk — newer, more uniform, purpose-built — do not have
Classes. The Bergfolk are watching the older constructs develop them and have not decided
how to feel about this.

### The Remnants — what the dead gods left

There are three confirmed Remnants active in Mistheim. They do not use the term. They
do not explain what they are. The word "Remnant" was coined by a Pandor [Scholar] in
the third century of the current age who needed something to write in the margin.

The first works as a [Messenger] for the Pandor scholars. It has done so for two hundred
years. It delivers things accurately and on time and does not discuss its nature.

The second sits at the Threefold Crossroads in the central highlands. It has been there
for longer than Vitstad has been inhabited. It causes no trouble. Travellers stop there,
sometimes, and ask it questions. It answers in a way that is technically accurate and
frequently unhelpful. Several of the answers have become proverbs.

The third has never been directly observed. Its passage is inferred from marks it leaves:
a particular pattern of Myst disturbance, a quality of silence in places where it has
recently been. The Fae appear to know more than they are saying about the third one.

### Visual identity — clothing, architecture, objects

This section is the reference for artists, sprite designers, and any lore agent writing
descriptions that a reader or player should be able to picture. Every Mistheim entry that
describes a character, location, or artefact should draw on these details.

The governing principle: **resources determine aesthetics.** What a people can make is what
they make themselves out of. What their land gives them is what shows up in their clothes,
their buildings, their tools. Nothing here is ornamental by accident.

---

#### Bergfolk

**Clothing:** Heavy and layered. Wool dyed with underground mineral pigments — ochre,
iron-red, deep charcoal. Over this, hardened leather aprons and shoulder guards with
metal rivets, not for combat but because sparks are real and the forge is everywhere.
Clasps and fastenings are always metal, usually over-engineered — a Bergfolk belt-clasp
is a small piece of craft in itself. Boots are stone-soled. Nothing worn underground
has bright colours; the eye adjusts.

**Architecture:** Deep-cut stone. Bergfolk buildings begin underground and grow upward
reluctantly — the external face is narrow and fortress-like, but the interior opens into
vaulted chambers lit by mana-granite panels that glow steadily without flame. Ceilings
are carved: not decoratively at first, but generations of craftspeople add to them, so
older chambers have intricate ceilings that took two hundred years to accumulate. Doors
are heavy, always functional, never decorative. The entrance to a hold looks like a wound
in the mountain.

**Objects:** Rune-carved tools — the runes are not decoration, they are the work record,
who made the tool and under what conditions. Mana-granite conducting blocks, sold in
standard sizes. Fired-clay cups with no handles (Bergfolk do not have a tradition of
handles). Notebooks bound in stone-pressed covers. A master [Runesmith]'s personal
tools are buried with them; taking someone's tools without permission is the closest
thing the Dyprike has to a taboo.

---

#### Lövfolk

**Clothing:** Light, layered, and dyed with forest pigments that shift in different lights —
a Lövfolk robe looks dark green at noon and deep blue at dusk. Fabric is woven from
processed bark-fiber and spider-silk, both of which the eastern forests produce in
quantity. Clothing moves like foliage: lots of layered panels, long sleeves, things that
catch the wind. Jewelry is living wood — small carved pieces that continue to grow slowly
if worn against skin, so a piece worn for thirty years looks different than it did when
it was given. Footwear is minimal; Lövfolk who spend time in the canopy are comfortable
barefoot on branch and rope.

**Architecture:** Nothing touches the ground if it can be helped. Lövfolk buildings are
suspended from the canopy on structural rope and living-wood brackets — the trees are
not cut but shaped over generations, their growth directed into load-bearing forms.
Buildings are round or oval, with no sharp angles. Windows face the canopy, not the
ground. The floor of a Lövfolk city is open air a hundred feet up, and the ground
below is quiet and dark and avoided. Older cities have buildings that have been shaped
for so long they are no longer distinguishable from the trees they grew from.

**Objects:** Myst-dense wood bows — not just any wood, specifically timber from trees
at the ley-line junctions, which the Lövfolk harvest with ceremony and sparingly.
Herbal compound vials sealed with living-wood stoppers that keep their potency for
decades. Living-tree archive chips: thin slices of the memory-trees, which a trained
[Keeper] can read by touch. The chips look like flat pieces of bark. They are worth more
than most cities.

---

#### Markfolk

**Clothing:** Practical and clay-dyed. Markfolk cloth is woven from river-grass and
cotton, dyed with the same mineral-rich clay that colours their pottery — terracotta,
river-blue, flood-plain brown. Broad hats are universal, almost a cultural marker:
wide-brimmed, woven from river grass, replaced at the start of each growing season.
Working clothes are belted; formal clothes are not. Metal is imported and used sparingly —
a Markfolk buckle is iron, not steel. [Watermasters] wear a specific braided cord on
the wrist that indicates their river assignment.

**Architecture:** Clay brick and river-stone. Buildings are low and wide with deep
overhanging eaves — the plains get weather, and the architecture has learned this.
Communal spaces are the centre of every settlement: the market square, the water-house,
the meeting hall where the river charts are kept. Private homes are peripheral; public
spaces are large. Everything is built to be added to — a Markfolk building is never
finished, it just has another room added when someone needs it. The oldest buildings in
a Merkförbund city have been added to for three hundred years and look like it, in a way
that is considered beautiful.

**Objects:** River-gauges — calibrated bronze rods planted at key points along managed
tributaries, read daily by [Watermasters]. Clay storage urns with pressed-in ownership
marks (the Markfolk equivalent of a label). Woven carry-baskets that fold flat. Seed
record books, kept obsessively: a serious Markfolk farmer knows the yield history of
their land going back six generations. A river chart drawn by a Markfolk [Watermaster]
is a work of functional art, annotated with current-patterns, flood-marks, and seasonal
notes in a hand so practiced it is almost calligraphy.

---

#### Viddfolk

**Clothing:** The Viddfolk are centaurs, so clothing addresses the upper body only. Light
woven panels across the chest and shoulders, in colours that correspond to their route
assignment — not ranks, exactly, but a visual code: a Herald who runs the eastern forest
route wears different accent colours than one who runs the northern mountain trade road.
The patterns are woven, not dyed separately, and the designs encode information: a skilled
reader can identify a Viddfolk's route, clan, and seniority from their clothing at a distance.
Mane and tail braiding carries additional information in a separate visual language.

**Architecture:** None permanent. The Speaking-Grounds have standing stones that mark the
boundaries and a fire-circle at the centre, but no roofs, no walls. Viddfolk temporary
shelter is constructed and dismantled in under an hour — a series of woven panels on
hinged poles that can be arranged in multiple configurations depending on weather and
group size. The quality of a temporary shelter reflects its owner's status: a senior
[Herald]'s shelter is indistinguishable from a permanent building for as long as it
stands.

**Objects:** Route-song tablets — flat carved panels with the encoded form of a memorised
route, used as teaching aids for young Viddfolk learning the network. These are not maps;
they encode the song, not the territory. Message cylinders: sealed scroll-cases that are
signed-and-sealed by the sender and broken-sealed by the recipient, with the Viddfolk
[Herald] as witness to both acts. Ley-line reading instruments: small devices that detect
Myst current strength and direction, worn on a cord — used to navigate the fastest route
between two points. Viddfolk [Heralds] treat these as intensely personal objects and do
not lend them.

---

#### Steinfolk

**Clothing:** Functional and stone-dusted, always. Work aprons in heavy canvas with
reinforced knees and elbows. Steinfolk formal clothing is still work clothing, just
without the dust — the same cut, the same material, cleaner. They do not have a separate
category of dress for ceremony. The dedication stone for a new project is laid in work
clothes; the funeral stone is placed in the same. Horns are not decorated in daily life
but are engraved at significant milestones — a Steinfolk [Engineer] who has completed a
major project adds a new mark to their horns. An elder's horns are extensively marked.

**Architecture:** Massive, load-bearing, built to outlast the builder. Steinfolk buildings
are never more ornate than the structure requires, but the structure is always precisely
right — a Steinfolk arch has no unnecessary material, but every stone is exactly where
it needs to be. Their strongholds are built at geographic problem-points: the buildings
exist because the location demanded something. The river-junction stronghold is built
around and over the river, incorporating the water into the structure so that the building
actively manages the flood-plain as it stands. Steinfolk architecture does not decorate
the outside. The interior is where the craft shows: fitted stone joints so clean they
need no mortar, load paths visible as aesthetic choices.

**Objects:** River-gauges (the Markfolk use the Steinfolk standard). Foundation stones
with inscribed dedication records — every major structure begins with a laid stone
inscribed with the date, the builder's name, the purpose of the building, and the names
of the people who commissioned it. These are not symbolic; they are structural. Leveling
instruments, calibrated to a standard maintained by the Femhörn council. A [Bridgekeeper]'s
travel kit: a set of testing tools for evaluating structural integrity, carried at all times.

---

#### Pandor

**Clothing:** Layered robes in bamboo-fiber weave — the fiber is processed to be lighter
than it looks, which it needs to be because Pandor scholars tend to carry things.
Scroll pockets are sewn into every garment, at multiple points. The dominant colours
are mist-grey, mineral-green, and aged ivory. Senior [Archivists] wear a specific collar
treatment that indicates their archive specialisation: blue-edged for cartography,
green for natural observation, red for historical record. Footwear is soft-soled for
quiet movement through archive spaces. Nothing on a Pandor is worn purely for appearance;
everything has a functional justification, even if the functional justification is
"indicates my rank so you know whether to defer to me."

**Architecture:** Bamboo-frame construction following the mist-line, built on terrace
systems that step up the highland slopes. The mist itself is a material — Pandor
kloster buildings are designed to channel and hold it, with vented interiors that
maintain the specific humidity required for the mineral-spring fermentation process.
Archive rooms are the most carefully constructed: stone-lined, mist-regulated,
with bamboo-framed shelves built to expand and contract with humidity rather than
warp against it. The exterior of a kloster looks modest; the interior is the most
controlled environment most visitors have ever been in.

**Objects:** Bamboo scrolls in fired-clay cases with mineral-spring ink that resists
water and mold better than most alternatives. The three-world tablet: a stone panel
inscribed in three parallel scripts — found in the ruins, reproduced in copies distributed
to all major kloster. Myst-reader instruments: devices that detect Myst flow direction
and strength, calibrated to Pandor standard and shared with nations that request them
(for a fee payable in information). A kloster's brewing equipment is maintained with the
same care as its archive equipment; the fermentation vessels are inscribed with the same
record-keeping practices as the scroll-cases.

---

#### Deepwalkers

**Clothing:** Salt-treated leather over close-woven fiber that dries fast. The salt
treatment gives Deepwalker leather a particular stiffness and smell — recognisable
at a distance. Colours are sea-grey, dark teal, and the particular faded blue that
comes from years of salt-water exposure. Deepwalker [Keepers] wear a specific braid
on the left wrist that encodes their clan and memory-lineage — not decoration but
identity documentation, readable by any other [Keeper]. Waterproofing is taken
seriously: seams are sealed with rendered oil, not just stitched. Footwear is either
heavy and waterproofed or absent.

**Architecture:** Stilt-construction over water or on tidal flats — everything built
to be moved if the sea demands it. Deepwalker buildings are modular: the wall panels
are interchangeable, the roof sections are removable, the floor sections are sealed
units that can be unlashed and rebuilt elsewhere. A stilt-town that has been in one
place for a century looks permanent; one that has been there for thirty years looks
like it arrived last week and may leave. The aesthetic that emerges from this is
spare and functional, but Deepwalkers decorate the one permanent element: the piling
anchors, which are carved with clan marks and tide records.

**Objects:** Salt-crystal preservation jars — fired clay with a salt-crystal glaze
inside that keeps fish and meat edible for months. Navigation memory-charts: not maps
but annotated memory records, written in a shorthand only trained [Navigators] read,
encoding how the sea behaves in specific conditions at specific points. Oral-memory
seal rings — worn by [Keepers], inscribed with the sealing-mark of every treaty in
their memory. A Deepwalker [Keeper] with thirty seals on their ring is a walking
legal archive. The rings are never transferred between keepers; they are buried with them.

---

#### Merfolk

**Clothing:** What the Djupvolk wear is not entirely accessible to surface observation,
since most contact happens at tidal points where lighting and conditions are poor.
What is visible: close-fitted materials that do not catch current, in bioluminescent
or reflective finishes — pale silver, deep iridescent blue, the colour of pressure-
crystal at depth. No loose fabric; nothing that would slow movement in water. Jewelry
is pressure-crystal set in lightweight metal, worn at the wrist and collar. The pieces
are small and functional — a pressure-crystal lens worn as a pendant has practical
uses for navigation in deep water.

**Architecture:** Not visible from the surface. What is known from the few accounts of
people who have been invited below: structures that use pressure differentials as load-
bearing elements, not stone and beam. Bioluminescent markers define the territory and
access paths. Living organisms incorporated into the buildings — the structure and the
ecology are not separated. The effect, according to those who have seen it, is that
entering a Djupvolk structure feels like entering a living body.

**Objects:** Pressure-crystal in worked and raw forms — the most recognisable Merfolk
trade good, identified by the specific refraction pattern that only forms under deep-
ocean conditions. Myst-kelp preparations in sealed tube containers, traded in small
quantities because the concentration degrades quickly above the thermocline. Objects
recovered from deep-sea ruins, cleaned and assessed — the Djupvolk do not always sell
these; sometimes they send them to the surface as messages about what they found and
what it might mean.

---

#### Goblins

**Clothing:** Layered, patched, and adapted from whatever was available. A goblin's
clothing is a history of the last several years — this piece from a Bergfolk hold,
this patch from Markfolk canvas, this fastening salvaged from a ruin. The layers
are functional: the outer layer protects and conceals, the inner layers insulate or
pad. Goblin [Delvers] add specific gear over this: knee-pads, elbow reinforcement,
a helmet that is clearly adapted from something else. Colours are whatever the
material was; goblin clothing is not dyed, because dye costs materials. The visual
effect is patchwork — and the specific patchwork, to a goblin, is readable as a
biography.

**Architecture:** Built into existing structures rather than beside them. Goblin
settlements in the ruins are structures-within-structures: walls added inside existing
walls, rooms created by adding a roof to a ruin's remaining three sides, passages
connecting spaces that were not previously connected. The result looks chaotic until
you understand the logic — every added element is solving a specific problem. Nothing
is decorative. Goblin [Menders] maintain everything; nothing that can be fixed is
abandoned. The settlements that have been in one place longest look like a collaboration
between six different construction traditions over two centuries, which is exactly
what they are.

**Objects:** Salvaged tools with added modifications — a Bergfolk hammer with a
grip re-wrapped for a smaller hand, a Deepwalker preservation jar repurposed for
carrying documents. Compact-seal tokens: small stamped metal discs that identify the
issuing band and function as letters of introduction within the Goblin Compact.
Carrying a valid token in the wrong band's territory is complicated; having none at all
is worse. Goblin maps: the most practically detailed maps of the ruin networks available,
drawn in compressed notation on salvaged material, often outdated and still more accurate
than anything a larger nation produces.

---

#### Fae

**Clothing:** The fae do not wear clothing in a material sense that maps onto other
peoples' experience. What is seen when someone encounters a fae is an impression of
clothing — usually something that fits the liminal space they occupy (a figure at
the forest edge in something that is simultaneously bark-brown and leaf-green; a fae
at the inn's back corner in something that looks like every piece of discarded fabric
that ever lay in a corner, somehow assembled). The impression shifts. Looking directly
at it is not recommended. What is consistent: fae do not wear anything uncomfortable,
and whatever they appear to be wearing is an expression of their current intent.

**Architecture:** Fae spaces do not obey consistent geometry. A room that should be
twelve feet across is forty feet across. A doorway leads to a different building than
the one its exterior is attached to. Time moves differently in established fae courts.
None of this is described as impossible by the fae themselves; it is described as
a different application of the same principles that make buildings work. The Hollow
Court spaces that have been in one location longest have the most stable geometry —
the Court of What Was's oldest chamber has only a fifteen percent size discrepancy,
which its residents consider practically normal.

**Objects:** The objects the fae carry are significant and indescribable in equal measure.
A fae who produces an object from somewhere on their person has produced it for a reason,
and the reason is part of the object. What can be said: fae objects tend to be old, tend
to be precisely the right size for their purpose, and tend to make the people who receive
them slightly uneasy even when the transaction was fair.

---

#### The Everstill

**Clothing:** Whatever they were wearing when they died, preserved but not updated.
The Everstill who died in the early period of Vitstad's founding wear clothing from
four centuries ago — the silhouettes and materials of previous ages, maintained in
good condition by people who have had four hundred years to learn textile preservation.
Newer Everstill wear more recent styles. The result, in a crowded room in the Pale
City, is a visible cross-section of Mistheim's fashion across the centuries. The
Everstill themselves do not find this strange. They find the living's tendency to
update their wardrobes seasonally faintly bewildering.

**Architecture:** Second-age architecture, intact. The Pale City is built from stone
that no current quarry can reproduce, in structural forms that no current [Engineer]
fully understands. The buildings have been maintained to their original condition —
the Everstill do not renovate, they restore. The effect is a city that looks finished,
which no living city does. No construction in progress. No scaffolding. No expansion.
The Pale City has exactly the buildings it had when its current residents arrived,
kept in the same state. It is the quietest city in Mistheim, not because its residents
are silent but because nothing is being built.

**Objects:** The archive, primarily: the collection of Vitstad's library. Individual
Everstill carry objects that were significant to them in life — a [Scholar]'s research
notes, an [Innkeeper]'s house-keys, a [Soldier]'s discharged equipment that was never
collected. These are not sentimental objects exactly. They are continuations of
interrupted work. The most requested item in the Vitstad archive, by visiting scholars,
is a technical document written by an Everstill [Engineer] who died mid-project in the
second age. The document ends mid-sentence. The [Engineer] is in the archive, still
working on what comes next. She has made significant progress in four centuries but
has not committed to a final answer.

---

#### Giants

**Appearance:** The seven are not uniform. Aldriksson is weathered granite-grey with a
beard that has accumulated enough moss and small plants over the centuries that it
constitutes a small ecosystem. Bergamora is darker, smoother-skinned, and moves with
a precision that suggests she has been paying close attention to how not to damage things
for a very long time. Hjelmfall has never been seen without a specific set of stones
that he carries, whose purpose and origin are not established. Each giant looks like
their territory: there is something of the mountain in Iskuldr, something of the coast
in Saltmara.

**Architecture:** Giants do not build in the conventional sense. What they leave is shaped
landscape: drainage patterns that redirect water, stone arrangements whose engineering
logic becomes apparent only when you see the territory from above. Aldriksson's valley
has one structure he made intentionally — a stone circle at the valley's centre that
he describes as a calendar. It is accurate to within three minutes per year.

**Objects:** Incomprehensible by scale and context. Aldriksson once gave a Pandor
delegation a stone he said was a record of something. The stone is six feet across.
Pandor [Keepers] have been attempting to read it for eighty years. Three of them believe
they have made progress.

### Alliances and political arrangements

**The Compact of Knowing** — Pandor, Lövfolk, Deepwalkers, and Vitstad. A knowledge-
sharing agreement with an attached legal framework. Non-military. Functions as the
continent's most significant soft-power arrangement because it controls access to the
most comprehensive historical record. Constructs are associate members by Pandor
classification; this is disputed.

**The Building Alliance** — Bergfolk, Markfolk, Steinfolk. A trade pact: ore for food,
engineering for materials, mutual support on infrastructure. The most economically
consequential alliance on the continent. Internal tensions (the foothills dispute, a
slow-building disagreement about river tributary rights) have not broken it in four
generations and probably won't. They need each other too much.

**The Old Accords** — Not an alliance. An agreement among all recognised nations (and
several unrecognised ones) to observe certain constraints: no warfare in Giant or Dragon
territory, no unsanctioned entry to Fae courts, no waking sleeping entities of uncertain
power. No one signed this. It was established by accumulated experience and is observed
because the cost of not observing it has been demonstrated enough times.

**The Vidde Accords** — A treaty recognising the Viddfolk's route-claims and their
neutrality. All major nations are signatories. The practical consequence is that the
Viddfolk communication network is treated as infrastructure everyone pays into and
nobody controls.

**The Goblin Compact's shadow network** — Not an alliance. An arrangement. The Compact
exchanges information with the Viddfolk [Heralds] and, indirectly, with the Pandor
logistics arm and certain Deepwalker merchants. Nobody has signed anything. Nobody
would admit to it in a diplomatic context. It is the most functional piece of pan-
continental intelligence sharing in Mistheim, operating entirely below the level of
official recognition.

### The Dry's political effect

The Dry is not evenly distributed. It follows the Myst — thinning where Myst pools
first, because those are the places where the flow disruption is most visible. This means
the peoples who depend most heavily on Myst are being affected first.

The Lövfolk enchantment exports are declining. The Viddfolk ley-line routes are slowing.
The Markfolk irrigation yields are starting to drop, quietly, at the margins. The Pandor
archives are going quiet in the living trees. The Deepwalker [Keepers]' memory Skills are
the first to be touched by corruption when it arrives.

The peoples least affected so far are those whose Myst relationship is indirect: the
Steinfolk, who build in stone and rely on engineering rather than enchantment; the
Markfolk's non-Myst farming, which still functions without the Myst-touch; the Merfolk,
whose deep-ocean currents are insulated from surface thinning; the Goblin Compact, which
never had reliable Myst access to lose.

This is shifting power in ways that have not yet been named as such:
- The Bergfolk's mana-granite is more valuable as an alternative to ambient Myst —
  but their own deposits are declining.
- The Merfolk's deep trade goods are suddenly premium.
- The Goblin Compact's ruins information has become economically significant rather than
  tolerated; sealed ruins are opening as Myst-locks weaken.
- The Everstill are discovering their condition may not be permanent.
- The fae deals struck under the old Myst concentrations are fraying.

The political response has been to not respond — each nation managing its own thinning
quietly, watching others for signs of weakness, none of them willing to be the first to
say the situation has a name. The Pandor have the records. They are still deciding.

## Cross-world notes for lore agents

- Each world has a **pre-corruption baseline** and a **corrupted state**. When writing a new
  entry, be clear (implicitly) which state you are describing.
- Corruption looks different on each world. On Earth: distortion and hyper-aggression.
  On Spinolandet: simplification and loss of symbiosis. On Vattenpandalandet: The Dry —
  the Myst thins and stops flowing. Magic stops working. Stories lose their endings.
  Skills hollow out — a [Healer]'s touch closes wounds that re-open, an [Innkeeper]'s
  warmth becomes cold hospitality with nothing behind it. The landscape dries as a
  symptom, not the cause.
- Characters from one world encountering another is rare and significant — do not write
  cross-world meetings casually.
- The player's presence in a zone does not automatically mean the zone is safe. Lore entries
  can describe ongoing danger even in cleansed areas.

## Peoples and creatures — reference for lore agents

### Safe to use freely

These are generic fantasy staples with roots in folklore, mythology, or public-domain sources.
They appear across dozens of works and are not associated with any single author's specific
treatment. Use them without restriction in Vattenpandalandet lore.

| Creature | Notes for this world |
|----------|----------------------|
| **Dwarves** | Mountain-dwellers, craftspeople, long memory for grudges and debts. They have Classes. [Runesmith] and [Stonemason] and [Brewmaster] are common. |
| **Elves** | Old, slow-ageing, often arrogant about it. Wide range — forest hermits, court advisors, wandering scholars. Their long lives make them indifferent in ways that read as cruel. |
| **Giants** | Rare. Ancient. Not necessarily aggressive — but scale changes what 'aggressive' means. Some have Classes; most have been around long enough to have outlived the relevance of their early levels. |
| **Goblins** | See Species section above. They have Classes. They are people. The world does not agree, and the world is wrong. |
| **Dragons** | Old powers, territorial, highly individual. Not 'evil' — they have goals that do not map onto human moral categories. Some have been asleep for centuries; waking them is a diplomatic incident with teeth. |
| **Undead** — liches, vampires, skeletons, revenants | Undeath interacts oddly with the Class system — some liches retain their Class, some lose it, some gain a new one. The system appears to have a [Lich] Class, though nobody knows who designed it or what it rewards. |
| **Fae / faeries** | Deal-makers. Older than the system. Their promises bind in ways the system cannot override, which suggests they predate it. Inns are one of the few places the fae will sit still for. |
| **Centaurs** | Territorial, honourable, with a complex relationship to the Class system — some reject it as an insult to their traditions; others have reached extraordinarily high levels in [Scout] and [Warrior] variants. |
| **Minotaurs** | Strong, loyal, given to ritual. Often found as [Guardians], [Soldiers], or [Craftspeople]. The labyrinth stories are considered culturally offensive by most minotaurs you would want to meet. |
| **Constructs / golems** | Animated objects given purpose. Some have developed Classes, which nobody fully understands. An ancient [Golem Guardian] that has levelled for centuries is not a thing to fight lightly. |
| **Merfolk** | People of coasts, rivers, and deep places. Often overlap with Deepwalker territory, and the relationship between the two groups is complicated and old. Some are [Scholars] of the pre-system ages. |
| **Orcs** | Warriors, shamans, clan-builders. Like goblins, the world prefers them as obstacles. Like goblins, they have Classes and memories and opinions about architecture. |
| **Angels / demons** | Rare. Tied to the dead gods — possibly literal remnants, possibly something the dead gods built. Neither term is self-applied. They do not explain themselves. |

### Avoid — entities too specifically associated with other works

These creatures have been so distinctively developed by their source authors that using them
would constitute lifting another writer's work, not borrowing from shared folklore:

| Entity | Source | Why to avoid |
|--------|--------|--------------|
| Antinium | *The Wandering Inn* | Six-limbed insectoid hivemind workers with suppressed individuality — pirateaba's specific invention |
| Spren | *The Stormlight Archive* | Emotion-born sapient forces tied to Surgebinding — Sanderson's specific magic system |
| Singers / Parshmen / Parshendi | *The Stormlight Archive* | Form-shifting people with stormlight physiology — deeply Sanderson-specific |
| Selphids | *The Wandering Inn* | Body-possessing people who inhabit corpses — pirateaba's specific treatment |
| Dullahans | *The Wandering Inn* | Pirateaba has a specific and extensive treatment; use elsewhere risks tonal confusion |
| Fraerlings | *The Wandering Inn* | Tiny people with world-specific culture — too specifically TWI |

General rule: if a creature's defining characteristics come from a specific author's magic
system, biology, or social structure, and not from folklore or mythology, don't use it.
If you are unsure, use a generic equivalent (e.g. 'construct' instead of Spren,
'small folk' instead of Fraerlings) and develop it from scratch.
